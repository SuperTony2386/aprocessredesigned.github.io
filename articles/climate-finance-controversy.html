Climate Finance Controversy: The Global Fight Over Broken Funding Promises

The global fight over how developed nations should fund climate adaptation in developing countries, with accusations of broken promises, insufficient targets, and systemic inequities.

By Tony Wolf March 28, 2026

The battle over climate finance has become one of the most contentious and defining issues in international climate negotiations. At the heart of this controversy lies a fundamental question of historical responsibility and global equity. Should wealthy nations that industrialized first and contributed the most to climate change bear the financial burden for helping developing countries adapt to climate impacts and transition to clean energy. The answer, according to international agreements, is yes. But the reality of how much money promised, how much actually delivered, and what counts as climate finance has sparked fierce disagreements and deep mistrust between the Global North and Global South.

The Broken Promise of $100 Billion

The story begins in Copenhagen in 2009, when developed countries pledged to mobilize $100 billion annually by 2020 for climate action in developing countries. This pledge was formalized in the Paris Agreement in 2015 and became a cornerstone of trust between wealthy and developing nations. For developing countries, this money was not charity. It was recognition that climate change caused primarily by emissions from industrialized nations was now devastating their economies, infrastructure, and communities through extreme weather, sea-level rise, and agricultural disruptions. The $100 billion was meant to help them adapt to these impacts and transition their economies away from fossil fuels without sacrificing development.

But 2020 came and went. The target was missed. According to data from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), developed countries mobilized only $83.3 billion in 2020. The following year in 2021, the figure rose to $91.4 billion. Finally, in 2022, developed countries reached $115.9 billion, exceeding the $100 billion goal for the first time. On the surface, this appears to be a success story a delayed but ultimately fulfilled promise. However, the numbers tell a more complicated story when examined closely.

Developing countries and civil society organizations have raised numerous critiques about how climate finance is counted and what actually reaches the ground. A key concern is that most of this finance comes in the form of loans rather than grants. According to analysis by the Heinrich Böll Foundation, in 2019 only 27 percent of climate finance was provided as grants, while 71 percent came as loans. This means developing countries are taking on debt to address a problem they did not create. Some of these loans are even provided at market rates, according to the foundation's analysis, which critics argue undermines the principle of climate justice. Loans need to be repaid with interest. They add to the debt burden of countries already facing economic challenges from climate impacts. For climate justice advocates, true climate finance should be predominantly grants, not debt-creating loans.

The composition of climate finance between mitigation and adaptation reveals another troubling disparity. Mitigation refers to actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions, like building renewable energy infrastructure. Adaptation refers to measures that help countries cope with climate impacts that are already occurring or unavoidable, like building sea walls, improving drought-resistant agriculture, or developing early warning systems for extreme weather. Vulnerable developing countries have emphasized that adaptation finance is essential for their survival. But the funding flows tell a different story. According to OECD data, in 2022 mitigation finance totaled $69.4 billion, while adaptation finance reached only $37.2 billion. About $9.3 billion funded activities that addressed both. This means roughly 60 percent of climate finance was directed toward mitigation, while only 32 percent was for adaptation.

This imbalance matters because mitigation projects, particularly renewable energy investments, often generate financial returns and can attract private investment. But adaptation projects typically do not generate profits. They are investments in public safety and resilience. The inability of many adaptation measures to attract private capital is precisely why developed countries should provide public grant financing. Yet the current system incentivizes mitigation over adaptation, leaving the most vulnerable communities underfunded. Small island developing states, least developed countries, and African nations have consistently emphasized that their priority is adaptation finance. They are on the front lines of climate impacts they did not cause. But the funding they receive remains inadequate for the scale of their needs.

Perhaps the most glaring omission from climate finance accounting is loss and damage. This refers to climate impacts that go beyond what adaptation can address, such as islands permanently lost to sea-level rise, catastrophic disasters that destroy entire communities, or slow-onset changes that make regions uninhabitable. For years, developed countries resisted including loss and damage in climate finance discussions, fearing it would open the door to unlimited liability claims. This exclusion meant that the most devastating climate impacts remained effectively unfunded. It was only after intense pressure at COP27 in 2022 that developed countries finally agreed to establish a loss and damage fund. But they made clear that this would be separate from and in addition to the $100 billion climate finance pledge. As of early 2025, the loss and damage fund remains operational but severely underfunded relative to the scale of needs.

COP29 and the Billion Dollar Betrayal

The controversy over climate finance continued at COP29 in Baku, where countries negotiated the New Collective Quantified Goal on climate finance to replace or supplement the expired $100 billion target. The outcome a pledge of $300 billion annually by 2035 was met with outrage from developing countries and civil society. Many countries walked out of negotiations in protest. Climate Action Network characterized the agreement as a "betrayal" of developing nations. So why was $300 billion, triple the previous target, so unacceptable to vulnerable countries.

The answer lies in what developing countries actually need versus what developed countries offered. The United Nations Environment Programme has estimated that developing countries will need between $215 billion and $387 billion annually for adaptation alone by 2030. When mitigation needs are included, the total annual climate finance needs for developing countries are projected to reach upwards of $2.4 trillion by 2030. Meanwhile, the Independent High-Level Expert Group on Climate Finance has suggested that $1 trillion per year in external financing is needed by 2030 for emerging markets and developing countries outside China. Against this backdrop of needs measured in trillions, an offer of $300 billion especially considering the accounting practices and loan-heavy composition of current finance appeared woefully inadequate.

The negotiations revealed profound disagreements between developed and developing countries about how the NCQG should be structured. Developed countries, particularly the United States and European Union, pushed for a broad, undefined goal that would include a wide range of financial flows, including private capital mobilized by public money. They argued that trillions of dollars in investment are needed from the private sector and that public finance alone cannot meet the scale of the challenge. Developing countries, led by the Like-Minded Developing Countries group and the African Group, argued for a strictly defined goal of public finance from developed to developing countries. They wanted clarity on grants versus loans, minimum percentages for adaptation, and the inclusion of loss and damage. They feared that without such definitions, the goal would become meaningless and developed countries would count nearly anything toward their commitments.

What emerged from COP29 was a compromise that satisfied almost no one. The text establishes a goal of at least $300 billion annually by 2035, with a call to work together to collectively scale up to $1.3 trillion per year from all sources. To developed countries, this represented a significant increase from the $100 billion target and an ambitious new goal. To developing countries, it was an insult. The $300 billion is undefined as to composition. It includes private finance. It makes no specific commitments on grants versus loans or minimum adaptation shares. The $1.3 trillion is aspirational and collective, requiring developing countries to mobilize their own finance and private capital, rather than representing a firm commitment from developed countries.

The timing of the goal and the way the numbers were presented also drew criticism. First, the target year is 2035, giving wealthy countries a decade to reach a level that many experts suggest they are already approaching with existing policies. Second, in nominal terms $300 billion in 2035 will have significantly less purchasing power due to inflation. According to analysis by Carbon Brief, depending on inflation projections, $300 billion in 2035 could be equivalent to just $175 billion to $217 billion in today's dollars. This is only marginally higher than the $115.9 billion already mobilized in 2022. When considering this adjustment, the proposed increase appears far less ambitious than the headline figure suggests.

Carbon Brief's analysis further revealed that with no additional effort beyond existing trajectories, developed countries could reach approximately $200 billion by 2030. If developing countries include their own voluntary climate finance contributions, which they have submitted through their Nationally Determined Contributions, an additional $36 billion could be added. This means developed countries claim to be offering a new target that is only modestly above what current trajectories would yield without new policies or commitments. From the perspective of developing countries, this is not an increase in ambition. It is simply rebranding business as usual as a new and improved goal.

Multilateral Development Banks and the Role of International Financial Institutions

Beyond direct bilateral finance between countries, a significant portion of climate finance flows through Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs) like the World Bank, Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, and Inter-American Development Bank. These institutions have increasingly oriented their lending toward climate projects in recent years, and developed countries count their contributions to MDB capital replenishments and concessional lending windows toward their climate finance commitments. However, the MDB approach to climate finance has its own complications and controversies.

MDB lending counts toward climate finance totals even when it consists of loans to developing countries. The World Bank and other MDBs emphasize that they provide concessional financing with more favorable terms than commercial loans. However, these loans still create debt obligations. For a developing country facing severe climate impacts that reduce economic output, taking on more debt for adaptation projects can create what critics call a debt trap. The country must service the debts while climate impacts undermine its economic capacity to pay. This is not sustainable climate finance. It is simply externalizing climate costs onto the balance sheets of vulnerable countries.

MDBs have also faced criticism for how they count climate finance, particularly the practice of attributing portions of general budget support or policy loans as climate finance. Even if a loan is intended for general development purposes, MDBs may estimate that a certain percentage supports climate-related outcomes and count that portion toward climate finance totals. This methodology inflation has led to accusations of cooking the books to make climate finance flows appear larger than they actually are. When the OECD reports that developed countries mobilized $115.9 billion in 2022, this figure includes such attributions and estimations that may not reflect actual new, additional, and clearly climate-tagged resources.

Recent reform efforts have sought to increase the scale of MDB climate lending. The World Bank has announced an "Evolution Roadmap" aiming to significantly increase its climate annual lending, with a target of providing 45 percent of its financing for climate-related projects by 2025. Similarly, the G20 Independent Expert Group has recommended that MDBs could unleash additional hundreds of billions in climate lending through balance sheet optimization and risk-taking approaches. While these increases are necessary, they do not resolve the fundamental question of whether loans constitute appropriate climate finance given the debt burden on vulnerable countries.

The Debt Crisis and Climate Finance

The controversy over climate loans versus grants cannot be separated from the broader context of debt distress in developing countries. Many climate-vulnerable nations are already struggling with unsustainable debt burdens, exacerbated by COVID-19 pandemic costs, rising food and energy prices, and the economic impacts of climate disasters. According to the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development, 52 developing countries were either in debt distress or at high risk of debt distress as of 2023. These countries account for 40 percent of the world's population living in poverty.

Africa, for example, spends more on debt servicing than it receives in climate finance. This is a perverse situation where the resources that should be helping countries adapt to climate change are instead flowing outward to creditors, many of whom are the same developed countries pledging climate finance. Civil society organizations have argued that this debt effectively cancels out climate finance inflows, leaving countries worse off. They call for debt relief and cancellation as part of climate finance, arguing it is fundamentally unjust that developing countries must service debts from loans often incurred for fossil fuel infrastructure or projects that contributed to climate change vulnerability, while simultaneously needing loans for climate adaptation.

Small island developing states face particular challenges. Their small economies and high exposure to climate impacts like hurricanes, sea-level rise, and coral reef bleaching make them highly vulnerable. Yet they often lack access to concessional financing due to middle-income status based on GDP per capita that does not reflect their vulnerability. For many small island states, the majority of climate finance they receive comes as loans. This creates a cycle where climate disasters damage infrastructure and economies, countries borrow to rebuild, debt servicing consumes limited revenue, and they remain unprepared for the next disaster.

The climate justice movement argues that this situation demands a fundamental transformation of climate finance. Rather than loans that create debt burdens, developed countries should provide substantial grant financing. They should also support debt restructuring, cancellation, and innovative financial instruments like debt-for-climate swaps. Developed countries that have historically benefited from fossil fuel-driven economic growth have a moral responsibility to ensure that the costs of transition and adaptation do not fall on those who benefited least from that growth.

Private Finance and the Flawed Mobilization Approach

A central tension in climate finance debates is the role of private capital. Developed countries consistently emphasize the need to mobilize private investment at scale to meet climate finance needs. They include private finance mobilized by their policies and contributions in their climate finance reporting. The OECD data, for example, shows that of the $115.9 billion mobilized in 2022, public climate finance from developed country governments accounted for $91.4 billion, while private finance mobilized by public interventions totals $20.6 billion.

The mobilization approach works like this. A developed country provides a relatively small amount of public finance, such as a grant, concessional loan, or guarantee, that de-risks a larger investment project. This public intervention helps attract private investors who might otherwise find the project too risky. The total investment is then counted toward the developed country's climate finance mobilization, even though the majority of the money comes from private investors seeking returns. There are several problems with this approach from the perspective of developing countries.

First, mobilized private finance is not additional public resources flowing from developed to developing countries. It is private capital investment that may have happened anyway or that would have gone elsewhere. While public intervention can help direct private capital toward climate projects, counting it as part of a developed country's climate finance obligation masks the limited scale of actual public resource transfers. Second, private finance mobilization is overwhelmingly skewed toward mitigation projects, particularly renewable energy infrastructure, because these generate revenue streams and investment returns. Private investors are generally not interested in financing adaptation projects that have no revenue potential. This means the mobilization approach exacerbates the mitigation-adaptation imbalance.

Third, the accounting methodologies for mobilized private finance are opaque and subjective. Different institutions use different multipliers to estimate how much private finance is mobilized by a given public intervention. There is no standardized, transparent methodology. This allows for questionable accounting that may inflate mobilization figures. Developing countries have argued that climate finance accounting should focus on public resources provided on grant and concessional terms, rather than private mobilization estimates that are ultimately not under the direct control or obligation of developed countries.

The Fight Over Definitions and Methodologies

Beneath the disagreements over dollar amounts lies a deeper battle over what counts as climate finance and how it should be measured. This battle plays out in the methodology discussions at UN climate negotiations, in the opaque working groups that determine OECD reporting standards, and in the technical reviews of climate finance data. For developed countries, flexible definitions and methodologies allow them to maximize the headline numbers they can report. For developing countries, these same flexibilities render the numbers meaningless and undermine trust.

One key fight is over the definition of climate finance. Should it only include finance that is new and additional, predictable, and provided on grant or concessional terms. These are the qualifiers originally included in the Copenhagen Accord but consistently ignored in practice. OECD reports present gross flows of finance without adjusting for whether this money represents new resources or simply repackaging existing development assistance. They make little distinction between grants and loans, public and private, or concessional and market-rate financing. This lumping together of very different types of financial flows makes the $115.9 billion figure appear to represent a certain level of effort when the actual public grant resources may be far smaller.

Another methodological controversy is the counting of export credits. Export credits are government-backed loans provided to companies so they can export goods and services to other countries. Some developed countries, including Japan and European nations, have argued that export credits for clean energy technologies should count toward climate finance. Developing countries vigorously oppose this. Export credits are designed to benefit companies in the exporting country, not to support climate action in developing countries. They are essentially subsidies for domestic industries dressed up as climate finance. Counting them is particularly egregious when many of these exports go to middle-income countries that already have access to capital markets, rather than to the most vulnerable least developed countries.

The OECD's role as the primary data collector on climate finance is also controversial. The OECD is an organization of wealthy, developed countries. It sets the methodologies and definitions for counting climate finance essentially from the perspective of its member countries. Developing countries cannot participate in setting these methodologies. They must simply accept the numbers presented to them. This situation has led to repeated calls for an independent, UN-based climate finance system with developing country participation in governance and methodology-setting. The Standing Committee on Finance under the UNFCCC has produced reports on climate finance flows, but the OECD data remains the primary source cited in negotiations. Developing countries argue this structural imbalance undermines the credibility of climate finance reporting.

Climate Justice and Historical Responsibility

At its core, the climate finance controversy is about climate justice. The principle of common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities, enshrined in the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change, recognizes that countries differ in their historical contributions to climate change and their capacity to respond. Industrialized countries became wealthy through fossil fuel-driven economic growth that emitted massive amounts of greenhouse gases over more than a century. Developing countries emitted relatively little during this period. Now developed countries have the financial resources, technology, and institutional capacity to address climate change, while developing countries face climate impacts that threaten their development prospects, security, and in some cases their very existence.

This historical context frames climate finance not as charity but as a matter of rights and obligations. Developed countries have a responsibility to provide finance to help developing countries mitigate emissions and adapt to climate impacts. This is not just about correcting past wrongs but about enabling a fair global transition. Without adequate climate finance, developing countries cannot pursue the low-carbon development paths they need. They face a cruel choice either continue economic growth based on fossil fuels and face catastrophic climate consequences, or attempt a clean energy transition without sufficient external support and face economic stagnation.

The climate justice movement emphasizes that adequate, predictable, grant-based climate finance is an obligation, not a favor. They point out that developed countries have mobilized trillions of dollars for other priorities. The United States alone spent over $2 trillion on its response to the 2008 financial crisis. European nations have provided hundreds of billions in response to energy crises. The COVID-19 pandemic saw trillions in economic stimulus globally. Against this backdrop, a commitment of $300 billion per year from all developed countries combined a goal that is aspirational rather than binding appears not just insufficient but insulting.

Vulnerable countries also raise the issue of reparations. Loss and damage beyond adaptation requires compensation. When islands permanently lose land to sea-level rise or when extreme weatherdestroy infrastructure, this is not merely an adaptation cost but an irrecoverable loss.Developed countries have historically resisted calls for reparations and compensation, fearing liability claims. But as climate impacts intensify, developing countries increasingly frame loss and damage financing as a matter of climate justice. They argue that polluters should pay for the damages they cause. This principle of polluter pays is well-established in environmental law and climate advocates argue it should apply internationally.

The Way Forward or More of the Same

As of early 2025, the climate finance system remains deeply flawed. The $100 billion target may have been technically exceeded in 2022, but the methodological questions and composition of that finance undermine the achievement. The new $300 billion target for 2035 has generated mistrust and anger rather than confidence. Loss and damage has made it onto the agenda as a funding stream separate from climate finance but remains underfunded. The debt crisis in developing countries continues to worsen, compounded by climate impacts and climate loans. The fundamental structural imbalances in the system remain unresolved.

Moving forward will require difficult conversations about what climate finance is actually for and who should pay. Civil society organizations are advocating for transformative approaches. These include much larger volumes of grant-based finance, a minimum 50 percent allocation for adaptation, debt cancellation mechanisms, dedicated loss and damage funding in the billions not millions, reform of MDBs to provide grants rather than loans, and transparent, accountable accounting with developing country participation in governance.

Some proposals suggest moving beyond voluntary contributions to a system of assessed contributions based on countries' economic capacity and historical emissions. This could generate far more predictable and adequate finance. Other proposals explore innovative financial instruments like Special Drawing Rights allocations, taxes on fossil fuels or financial transactions, and climate-related trade measures. But these proposals face strong resistance from developed countries that prefer voluntary pledges and private mobilization approaches that limit their direct financial obligations.

What is clear is that business as usual is not sufficient. The gap between climate finance needs and flows is widening, not narrowing. Climate impacts are accelerating faster than adaptation capacity. Vulnerable countries are falling further behind in the clean energy transition. The current system encourages creative accounting rather than real delivery of resources. It prioritizes mitigation over adaptation and loans over grants. It excludes the very concept of loss and damage for too long. Without fundamental reform to address these flaws, climate finance will continue to be a source of conflict rather than cooperation in the global climate regime.

The technical debates over definitions and methodologies matter because they determine whether climate finance is a meaningful instrument for climate justice or a rhetorical cover for insufficient action. When developed countries claim progress while providing mostly loans that create debt burdens, they are not fulfilling their obligations. When vulnerable countries receive half the finance they need for adaptation while their communities suffer climate disasters, the system is failing. When the world celebrates exceeding a $100 billion target while ignoring that most of this money is not new, additional, or adequately targeted, we are witnessing the triumph of optics over substance.

The climate finance controversy will continue to define international climate negotiations. It sits at the intersection of equity, responsibility, and trust. Without resolving it, the global climate regime will struggle to achieve its goals. Adequate and predictable climate finance is not just a necessary condition for climate action in developing countries. It is a test of whether the international community can genuinely address a global collective action problem that requires some countries to provide more than they receive for the common good.
