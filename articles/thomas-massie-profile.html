Thomas Massie: A Fact-Based Profile and Voting Record

A detailed examination of Congressman Thomas Massie's background, legislative positions, voting record, and the unheard-of primary challenge he faces in 2026
By Tony Wolf May 16, 2026 20-Minute Read
Share This Investigation Twitter Facebook Email

Thomas Harold Massie has served as the United States Representative for Kentucky's 4th congressional district since November 2012. A member of the Republican Party, Massie has established a distinct record in Congress characterized by frequent opposition to legislation from both parties, consistent votes against government spending measures, and positions that often place him at odds with his own party's leadership. This article presents factual information about Massie's background, legislative positions, voting record, and notable actions, drawing from official sources, congressional records, and media coverage.

Background and Early Career

Massie was born on January 13, 1971, in Huntington, West Virginia, and was raised in Vanceburg, Kentucky, in Appalachian Kentucky. He earned a Bachelor's degree in electrical engineering in 1993 and a Master's degree in mechanical engineering in 1996 from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). His master's thesis focused on haptic interfaces and virtual touch technology.

In 1993, Massie and his first wife, Rhonda Howard, founded SensAble Devices Inc., which became SensAble Technologies Inc. The company developed technology allowing users to feel digital objects on screens, securing 24 patents and raising $32 million in venture capital. Massie won the $30,000 Lemelson-MIT Student Prize for inventors in 1995. He sold the company in 2003.

Massie entered public service in 2010 when he ran for Judge-Executive of Lewis County, Kentucky. He won both the Republican primary and the general election, serving from January 2011 until July 2012, when he resigned to run for Congress.

Massie's first wife, Rhonda Howard Massie, died in 2024. The couple had four children together. He married Carolyn Moffa in 2025.

Congressional Career

Massie was elected to Congress in November 2012 in a special election to replace Congressman Geoff Davis, who resigned from office. Massie won both the special election to complete Davis's term and the regular election for a full two-year term on the same day. He has been re-elected multiple times.

Upon arriving in Congress, Massie was assigned to serve on three committees: Transportation and Infrastructure; Oversight and Government Reform; and Science, Space, and Technology. In the current 118th Congress, he serves on the Committee on Rules, the Committee on the Judiciary, and the Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure. He chairs the House Judiciary Subcommittee on the Administrative State, Regulatory Reform, and Antitrust.

Massie is a member of the Second Amendment Caucus and the Liberty Caucus, and his voting record has been described as libertarian-conservative in orientation. During the 117th Congress, Massie voted with President Joe Biden's stated position only 1.8% of the time according to a FiveThirtyEight analysis, tying him with Representative Chip Roy as the least likely member of Congress to vote with the president on any given issue.

Voting Patterns and Legislative Record

Spending and Debt

Massie has consistently opposed measures that increase government spending or raise the debt ceiling. He was the sole "no" vote on numerous pieces of legislation and has voted against major spending bills passed by both Republican and Democratic administrations.

In 2025, Massie voted against the One Big Beautiful Bill Act and the Full-Year Continuing Appropriations and Extensions Act, arguing both bills were not sufficiently fiscally conservative. In response to these votes, President Donald Trump announced he would support and fund a primary challenger against Massie's re-election campaign. On March 11, 2026, Trump visited Verst Logistics in Hebron, Kentucky, within Massie's district, to rally support for primary challenger Ed Gallrein, stating during his speech that Massie "has got to be voted out of office as soon as possible" and giving Gallrein his "complete and total endorsement."

Massie has cited the U.S. national debt as a primary concern. In 2023, he created the "Debt Badge," a wearable device that displays the national debt in real-time. He reportedly wears it constantly while in Washington, D.C., only removing it to sleep, stating that he designed it to "put the debt in the face of (his) colleagues every day."

Foreign Policy and Foreign Aid

Massie has established a record of opposing foreign aid packages and military intervention abroad. In an April 2026 interview with 77 WABC, Massie called for ending all foreign aid, stating that American taxpayers should not fund foreign governments through foreign aid extracted through inflation and domestic taxes. On Instagram that same week, Massie posted about submitting three amendments to a spending bill that he said would save taxpayers $6.9 billion by cutting foreign aid: $3.3 billion from Israel, $2.1 billion from Jordan, and $1.5 billion from Egypt.

Massie has repeatedly voted against foreign aid to Israel. In September 2021. He was the only Republican to vote against $1 billion of funding for Israel's Iron Dome missile defense system. In October 2023. He was the only Republican to vote against a House resolution guaranteeing U.S. support for Israel's military actions in Gaza. In November 2023. He was the sole member of Congress to oppose a resolution affirming Israel's right to exist and equating anti-Zionism to antisemitism. In July 2024, Massie boycotted Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's address to Congress, calling it "political theater"; he was the only Republican to do so.

Massie has been publicly critical of the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) and its influence on American politics. In December 2023, he tweeted out a meme appearing to contrast Zionism with American patriotism. In May 2024, AIPAC and allied groups announced a $300,000 ad campaign targeting Massie for perceived "anti-Israel views" while not officially endorsing any primary challenger. Massie responded by posting a poll on X asking his followers whether AIPAC should be forced to register as a foreign agent under the Foreign Agents Registration Act. According to Massie, 94.9% of respondents voted "Yes, register under FARA."

Regarding Ukraine, Massie was one of only three members of Congress to oppose a March 2022 resolution supporting Ukraine's sovereignty after Russia's invasion. He opposed a resolution in 2022 to support Sweden and Finland joining NATO, saying he did not want to "subsidize socialist Europe's defense." In March 2024, he voted against House Resolution 149 condemning "the wrongful and illegal kidnapping of children from Ukraine" by Russia, one of nine Republicans to do so.

On Iran, Massie voted "present" on the 2015 Iran nuclear agreement, the only member of the House to do so and the only Republican to not vote against it. He was the only member of the House to vote against extending sanctions on Iran in 2016.

Civil Liberties and Government Surveillance

Massie has been a consistent critic of government surveillance programs. In 2014, he sponsored an amendment to stop warrantless "backdoor" searches of U.S. citizens' online data; the amendment passed the House 293-123. The amendment also contained a provision prohibiting the NSA or CIA from requesting companies to install surveillance backdoors in their products.

In 2015, Massie introduced the Surveillance State Repeal Act, a bill that sought to repeal the PATRIOT Act and the FISA Amendments Act. That same year, he joined with Representative Justin Amash in an effort to ensure the expiration of certain provisions of the PATRIOT Act.

Massie has called for National Security Agency whistleblower Edward Snowden to be pardoned and for Director of National Intelligence James Clapper to be prosecuted for lying to Congress while under oath about the phone metadata program that Snowden exposed. In April 2026, Massie and Representative Lauren Boebert co-introduced the Surveillance Accountability Act, which seeks to establish stricter legal thresholds for government surveillance by requiring warrants for certain searches and restricting the warrantless use of facial recognition systems and automated license plate readers.

Gun Rights

Massie has introduced and supported multiple pieces of legislation eliminating gun control measures. During the 2019-2020 session, Massie introduced H.R. 2071, the "Second Amendment Protection Act," which would lift the federal prohibition on medical marijuana patients from owning or possessing firearms.

In 2022, Massie introduced H.R. 7415, the "Safe Students Act," which would repeal the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990, effectively repealing the federal ban on guns in school zones and allowing state and local governments and school boards to set their own firearms policies. Massie has argued that gun-free zones are ineffective and make schools less safe, citing statistics that mass public shootings predominantly occur in areas where citizens are banned from having guns.

Massie has criticized President Joe Biden's plans to regulate privately made firearms by reclassifying gun kits as firearms under the Gun Control Act and requiring manufacturers to be licensed and inscribe serial numbers on gun kits. On March 26, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled 7-2 in favor of the Biden administration's reclassification in an opinion authored by Justice Gorsuch.

Epstein Files Transparency

In July 2025, Massie introduced a discharge petition alongside Democratic Representative Ro Khanna to force a vote on a law to compel the release of Epstein files. President Trump criticized Massie for this action, calling him an "Embarrassment to Kentucky." The discharge petition passed on November 12, 2025, and the Epstein Files Transparency Act was signed into law a week later. The files were required to be released by December 19, 2025, but the Department of Justice only released part of the files that day and stated it had fulfilled its legal obligations, releasing what it said were all available files amounting to over 3.5 million pages.

In February 2026, Massie reviewed unredacted Department of Justice files related to Epstein alongside Representative Ro Khanna. Following the review, Massie stated that six individuals whose names had been redacted in publicly released materials were identified in the documents. He criticized the Justice Department's redactions and called for greater transparency. Among the names released by Khanna were Les Wexner and Sultan Ahmed bin Sulayem. According to reporting by The Guardian, the Department of Justice said that four of the men Khanna named had no apparent connection to Epstein whatsoever, but rather appeared in a photo lineup assembled by the southern district of New York.

COVID-19 Pandemic Response

On March 27, 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, Massie forced the return to Washington of House members who were "sheltering in place" in their districts by threatening a quorum call that would require an in-person vote on the $2.2 trillion Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act that had passed the Senate by a 96-0 vote. Before Massie arrived, only two representatives were present to pass the bill by voice vote. On the House floor, Massie stated that he was trying to "make sure our republic doesn't die by unanimous consent in an empty chamber."

Massie received criticism from multiple officials for requiring members of Congress to gather during the pandemic. President Trump said Massie should be removed from the Republican Party, calling him a "third rate grandstander." John Kerry remarked that Massie "tested positive for being an asshole." Representatives Sean Patrick Maloney and Dean Phillips criticized Massie's actions. Some Republicans defended Massie: Representative Paul Gosar called him a "good man and a solid conservative," and Representative Chip Roy said Massie was "defending the Constitution today by requiring a quorum."

In an interview with Politico, Massie stated: "The fact that they brought all of these congressmen here in order to get a quorum shows you that I was right. The Constitution requires a quorum to pass a bill, and they were planning to subvert the Constitution." He also questioned why people such as grocery store employees or truck drivers should be expected to work during the pandemic but not members of Congress.

In April 2020, Massie was one of five House members to vote against the Paycheck Protection Program and Health Care Enhancement Act, which added $320 billion of funding for the Paycheck Protection Program. In July 2020, he argued against face mask mandates and compulsory vaccinations. He faced allegations of antisemitism after comparing vaccine mandates to the Holocaust on Twitter, and later deleted the tweet.

On June 27, 2021, Massie, along with Representatives Marjorie Taylor Greene and Ralph Norman, sued Speaker Nancy Pelosi after they were fined for refusing to wear masks on the House floor.

Environment

Massie does not find the scientific evidence for climate change compelling. He has stated: "I think the jury is still out on the contribution of our activities to the change in the earth's climate." In 2013, he tweeted that a Science Committee hearing on global warming was canceled due to snow. During a 2019 House Oversight Committee hearing on the impact of climate change, Massie suggested that concerns over rising carbon dioxide levels were exaggerated.

Massie supports dismantling the Environmental Protection Agency. He voted to block the Department of Defense from spending on climate adaptation and voted to repeal the Stream Protection Rule, which imposed stricter requirements on coal mining to prevent coal debris from entering waterways.

In 2018, after French President Emmanuel Macron spoke to Congress and mentioned his desire that the United States rejoin the Paris Climate Accords to curb climate change, Massie described Macron as "a socialist militarist globalist science-alarmist. The dark future of the American Democratic Party."

Other Notable Votes

Massie has frequently been the sole or one of very few "no" votes in Congress. In March 2014, he voted against the Stolen Valor Act of 2013, which passed 390-3. In December 2013, he was the only congressman to vote against the Undetectable Firearms Act.

In May 2014, Massie forced a roll call vote on a voice vote to award golf star Jack Nicklaus a gold medal; the vote passed 371-10. Through mid-June 2014, Massie had voted "no" at least 324 times in the 113th Congress, opposing approximately one of every three measures that came to the House floor. Politico named him "Mr. No."

In November 2016, Massie voted against an extension of U.S. sanctions against Iran, the only member of the House to do so. In May 2017. He was the sole House member to vote against sanctions on North Korea.

In 2021, Massie was one of 14 Republicans to vote with all House Democrats to override Trump's veto of a measure unwinding Trump's declaration of a national emergency at the southern border.

In May 2022, Massie was the only member of the House of Representatives to vote against a non-binding resolution denouncing antisemitism and opposition to Israel. Massie tweeted that he voted against the bill because it promoted censorship.

Massie was the sole member of Congress to vote against the Hong Kong Human Rights and Democracy Act of 2019 and the UIGHUR Act in December 2019. Massie clarified on Twitter that his reasoning was that it is not the role of the United States to intervene in other nations' internal affairs.

On February 26, 2020, Massie voted against making lynching a federal hate crime. On February 28, 2022. He was one of three representatives to vote against the Emmett Till Antilynching Act.

Legislative Proposals

Massie has sponsored various legislative proposals. In 2014, he introduced the Milk Freedom Act and the Interstate Milk Freedom Act to allow the transportation of raw milk across state borders. In 2015, he introduced the Processing Revival and Intrastate Meat Exemption (PRIME) Act to ease federal regulations regarding the sale of meat within state borders.

In 2013, Massie introduced Justice Safety Valve Act to provide judges with greater sentencing flexibility. He has criticized civil asset forfeiture laws, calling them "legal robbery" and "completely unconstitutional," and helped introduce the Fifth Amendment Integrity Restoration Act to reform federal asset forfeiture policies in 2019.

Massie has introduced legislation to audit the Federal Reserve multiple times, including the Federal Reserve Transparency Act. He has supported efforts to repeal the Authorization for Use of Military Force of 2001, arguing that it is too broad and that Congress should reclaim its constitutional responsibility to declare war.

No piece of legislation proposed by Massie has been passed by Congress.

2026 Primary Challenge

Following Massie's votes against spending bills supported by President Trump in 2025, Trump announced he would support and fund a primary challenger against Massie. The 2026 Republican primary in Kentucky's 4th congressional district has become the most expensive U.S. House primary in history. Massie faces challenger Ed Gallrein, whom Trump endorsed during a March 11, 2026 rally at Verst Logistics in Hebron, Kentucky.

Media coverage in May 2026 reports that Massie has received significant fundraising support since Trump's announcement of opposition. Fox News reported that Massie "raked in more than $1M" in campaign contributions in a single week while fighting for his political life. CNN and other outlets have described this as "the toughest election of (Massie's) career." Coverage by the Daily Press notes that Kentucky Republicans historically strongly support Trump, raising questions about whether they will ignore Trump's opposition and re-elect Massie.

Scoring by Conservative Organizations

Various conservative organizations have scored Massie's voting record. Heritage Action scorecards show Massie receiving scores of 71% in the 115th Congress, 92% in the 116th Congress, 96% in the 117th Congress, and 70% in the 118th Congress. The drop in the 118th Congress reflects Massie's opposition to spending measures supported by House leadership and the Trump administration.

Gun Owners of America has given Massie high ratings for his record on Second Amendment issues. NumbersUSA, which seeks to reduce both legal and illegal immigration, gave Massie a 56% score in 2024. The Federation for American Immigration Reform, which also supports immigration controls, gave him a 92% rating in 2019-2020.
