The Decline of the United States: Global Disapproval and Domestic Distrust

An in-depth analysis of America's declining global influence and domestic institutional trust, from international and domestic perspectives
By Tony Wolf May 9, 2026 22-Minute Read
Share This Investigation Twitter Facebook Email

In a time of unheard-of global transformation, the United States faces a growing crisis of confidence both at home and abroad. Once the undisputed leader of the post-World War II order, America now navigates a environment where its global image has frayed, domestic trust in institutions has plummeted, and the unipolar world it dominated for decades has given way to an uncertain multipolar reality. This decline represents not merely a shift in power dynamics but a fundamental questioning of American exceptionalism and its future role in global affairs.

The International Erosion of American Influence

The United States' standing on the global stage has deteriorated significantly in recent years, with data from Pew Research Center revealing troubling trends across 24 countries surveyed in 2025. The research, encompassing 28,333 respondents, found that America's global image declined in 15 of these nations, particularly in Europe and among traditional allies. Just 38% of international respondents express confidence in President Donald Trump to do the right thing regarding world affairs, while 31% express little to no confidence, a concerning figure for a nation that once commanded broad international respect.

This skepticism extends beyond leadership to broader questions about American democracy itself. Half of those surveyed across the 24 countries say democracy in the United States works not very well or not at all, a stunning indictment of America's ability to serve as a model for democratic governance. Among developed nations, only in Japan does a majority of people say American democracy works well, while in countries like France, Germany, and the United Kingdom, less than half share this assessment. The implications for America's soft power are deep, when the world questions whether American democracy functions effectively, the moral authority the United States has long claimed as the leader of the free world becomes increasingly tenuous.

The European Union's perspective reflects a growing belief that the world has entered a "post-Western" era. European citizens, particularly following Trump's return to power, increasingly view China as a rising power while questioning America's commitment to allies and multilateral institutions. The European Council on Foreign Relations has documented how European leaders now openly discuss "strategic autonomy" from the United States, a formulation that would have been unthinkable during the Cold War or even the immediate post-9/11 period. NATO, once the cornerstone of transatlantic security, faces existential questions about its future relevance as European nations question American reliability under Trump's "America First" foreign policy.

In Asia, the picture varies but shares common threads of concern. Traditional allies like Japan and South Korea worry about American commitment to regional security, particularly given Trump's skepticism of traditional alliances and his transactional approach to foreign policy. Meanwhile, China's Belt and Road Initiative has expanded its influence across Asia, Africa, and Latin America, offering an alternative development model that challenges American economic dominance. The Group of 20's expansion to include emerging economies reflects this rebalancing, as does the BRICS bloc's consideration of admitting new members that would represent over 40% of the global population and a larger share of global GDP than the G7.

Domestic Collapse of Institutional Trust

Perhaps even more alarming than international skepticism is the deep loss of trust among Americans themselves. According to the Partnership for Public Service's 2025 report on public trust, only 33% of Americans trust the federal government to do what's right, matching historic lows reached during previous periods of crisis. More troubling still, 67% of Americans see the government as corrupt, a dramatic increase from previous decades and a figure that suggests growing cynicism about the very possibility of honest governance.

This distrust extends across institutions that form the bedrock of American society. Gallup polling reveals that confidence in the Supreme Court has reached record lows following controversial decisions and accusations of political bias. Trust in Congress remains mired in single digits, reflecting deep partisan divisions and paralysis. Even the media, traditionally a fourth estate holding power accountable, suffers from record-low confidence ratings at just 28%, as Gallup's 2024 data demonstrates. The erosion of trust in these institutions creates a vicious cycle: when citizens don't trust institutions, institutions struggle to function effectively, which further erodes trust.

The partisan divide in institutional trust represents a particularly dangerous development. Republicans and Democrats increasingly occupy different factual universes, with Republicans expressing much lower trust in government when Democrats control it and vice versa. This partisan polarization means that basic governance has become a political football, with neither party willing to trust the other to exercise power responsibly. The result is gridlock, political violence, and a growing belief among Americans that democracy itself may be failing.

Pew Research's data on Americans' views of democracy reveals a paradox: while most Americans still believe in democracy in principle, a growing portion questions whether it actually works in practice. Nearly half of Americans express concerns about the future of American democracy, with significant percentages believing that significant changes are needed. The January 6, 2021, Capitol attack, while now several years in the past, continues to loom large as an indication of how political polarization can explode into violence. Pew's 2025 research on political violence finds that most Americans say politically motivated violence is increasing, though they disagree on why it's happening and who bears responsibility.

Economic Challenges and Infrastructure Decline

The physical and economic foundations of American power also show signs of strain. The American Society of Civil Engineers' 2025 report card gives the United States' infrastructure a grade of C-, estimating a $2.6 trillion investment gap over the next decade alone. Roads, bridges, water systems, and energy infrastructure that once made America the envy of the world now rank below those of many European nations and even some developing countries. This decline has real economic consequences: the World Economic Forum's competitiveness rankings show the United States slipping in infrastructure quality, affecting business operations and quality of life.

The infrastructure gap becomes particularly stark when comparing American investments to those of China. While the United States struggles with partisan gridlock over infrastructure spending, China has invested roughly 10 times more in infrastructure over the past two decades, building high-speed rail networks, modern ports, and renewable energy systems that far exceed American capabilities. This disparity isn't merely about concrete and steel, it signals deeper issues with America's political system, fiscal priorities, and willingness to make long-term investments over short-term political calculations.

Income inequality presents another economic challenge undermining America's social fabric. The United States has one of the highest Gini coefficients among developed nations, measuring around 0.48 in 2024, according to U.S. Census Bureau data. This level of inequality exceeds that of most European countries, Canada, Japan, and other developed peers, creating social divisions that contribute to political polarization and distrust. The wealth gap has implications for social mobility, the American Dream of rising economic opportunity appears increasingly out of reach for many, with intergenerational mobility lower in the United States than in many other developed countries.

The inequality challenge intersects with other social problems. Higher inequality correlates with lower social trust, poorer health outcomes, reduced educational attainment, and greater political instability. As middle-class jobs disappear due to automation and globalization, and as the cost of housing, healthcare, and education outpaces wage growth, many Americans feel the system is rigged against them. This perception, whether accurate or not, feeds the broader crisis of trust in institutions and belief in the American economic model.

The Geopolitical Transition: From Unipolar to Multipolar

The post-Cold War period of American unipolar dominance has definitively ended, replaced by an emerging multipolar world order. This transition was accelerated by several factors: the disastrous 2003 Iraq War damaged American credibility and revealed the limits of military power; the 2008 financial crisis, originating on Wall Street, shook confidence in the American economic model; and China's rapid economic rise, combined with Russia's assertive foreign policy under Vladimir Putin, created new centers of power that no longer defer to American leadership.

The BRICS alliance, Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa, formally established its New Development Bank in 2015, creating an alternative to Western-controlled financial institutions like the World Bank and International Monetary Fund. The group's potential expansion to include other major economies like Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and Argentina would represent a direct challenge to Western economic hegemony. These developments reflect a growing rebellion against what many in the Global South perceive as American and European dominance of international institutions.

China's rise represents the most significant challenge to American primacy. With the world's second-largest economy by nominal GDP and the largest by purchasing power parity, China has extended its influence through the Belt and Road Initiative, the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, and increasingly assertive foreign policy in the South China Sea and elsewhere. While China faces its own economic and demographic challenges, its trajectory suggests a world where American dominance coexists with, if not yields to, Chinese power in Asia and beyond.

The European Union, meanwhile, grapples with its own questions about autonomy and alignment. While most European nations remain committed to NATO and the transatlantic alliance, there are growing calls for "strategic autonomy" that would allow Europe to act independently of the United States, particularly in areas where American and European interests diverge. The EU's emphasis on climate action, digital regulation, and multilateral governance contrasts with American approaches under Trump, highlighting values-based as well as strategic differences.

Conservative Perspectives: Denial of Decline or Call to Renewal?

The picture of American decline looks different through conservative eyes, and understanding these perspectives is critical for grasping the full complexity of the situation. Many conservatives reject the narrative of decline outright, arguing that talk of American weakness is itself a form of pessimism that undermines national confidence. From this perspective, America's fundamental strengths, economic dynamism, military power, democratic institutions, and cultural influence, remain unmatched, and what looks like decline is merely a period of inevitable transition and adjustment.

Conservatives who acknowledge challenges often frame them differently than liberals or international critics. Rather than seeing America's problems as systemic, they often blame specific policies, cultural trends, or domestic enemies. The "America First" agenda championed by Trump and his supporters represents one such framing, the idea that if America stopped spending so much on foreign aid, defense alliances, and international institutions, it could focus its resources on domestic priorities and regain its strength. This view portrays decline not as inevitable but as a consequence of misguided priorities by previous administrations.

Some conservative thinkers, particularly at institutions like the Cato Institute, argue that American decline stems from abandonment of founding principles rather than loss of relative power. The Cato Institute's "The Lost Liberalism of America First" argues that the original conservative vision of limited government, free markets, and non-interventionist foreign policy has been corrupted by big-government conservatism and imperial overreach. From this perspective, the path to renewal requires returning to these traditional principles rather than accepting a narrative of permanent decline.

However, there are also conservative voices who sound alarm bells about cultural decline, arguing that the real threat to America isn't external competition but internal decay. Social conservatives point to declining religious attendance, family instability, rising secularism, and cultural conflict as evidence that America has lost its moral compass. This perspective often overlaps with nationalist and populist rhetoric that frames decline in civilizational terms, requiring a return to traditional values and national greatness.

Perhaps the most conservative perspective comes from America First proponents who argue that decline is actually a choice, that if Americans simply stopped apologizing for their country, asserted national interests more boldly, and rejected globalist constraints, American power would be renewed. This view, common among Trump supporters, treats the appearance of decline as evidence of weak leadership rather than structural problems. The solution, from this perspective, isn't to adapt to a multipolar world but to restore American unipolarity through strength and will.

Liberal and International Perspectives: Structural Challenges

Liberals and international observers tend to frame American decline in more structural terms, seeing it as the product of deep-seated problems that require fundamental reforms rather than simply a change in leadership or attitude. From this perspective, the challenges facing America, political polarization, institutional distrust, infrastructure decay, inequality, and global competition, reflect systemic issues that have developed over decades and won't be easily resolved.

Liberal analyses often point to America's political system as a source of weakness. The Senate gives disproportionate power to small, rural states; the Electoral College can produce presidents who lose the popular vote; and Senate gridlock has become normalized. These features, designed for a different era, now produce dysfunction that prevents addressing major challenges. Campaign finance, Supreme Court legitimacy, and the influence of money and special interests further undermine the system's ability to deliver good governance.

Economically, liberal perspectives often emphasize structural inequality and the concentration of economic power. The decline of labor unions, the financialization of the economy, and the rise of monopoly power have contributed to a situation where economic gains increasingly go to the top 1% while wages for most Americans stagnate. This concentration of wealth translates into political power, creating a self-reinforcing cycle where wealthy interests shape policy to their benefit while the majority struggles.

Internationally, liberal voices often argue that American decline stems largely from self-inflicted wounds: the disastrous Iraq War, the 2008 financial crisis, the rise of populist nationalism under Trump, and the abandonment of multilateral institutions and alliances. Brookings Institution scholars have documented how Trump's "America First" approach has actually made China great again by creating vacuums of leadership that Beijing has eagerly filled. From this perspective, the path to renewal requires recommitting to alliances, multilateral institutions, and cooperative approaches to global problems.

European perspectives, while often critical, are not uniformly hostile. Many European leaders recognize that American power remains indispensable to maintaining global order and that European strategic autonomy, while desirable, has limits. The challenge, from this view, is not replacing American power but reforming it, creating a more collaborative, multilateral approach where Europe has a greater voice and America exercises power more consensually rather than unilaterally. This perspective frames decline less as inevitable and more as a necessary rebalancing toward a more equitable international system.

Independent Media and Alternative Perspectives

Beyond the mainstream conservative-liberal divide, independent media sources offer additional perspectives on American decline that challenge orthodoxies from both sides. Some independent voices, particularly from the political left, argue that American decline is actually a positive development that could create space for more just and sustainable global arrangements. From this perspective, American hegemony has enabled exploitation, military intervention, and environmental destruction, and multipolarity, while presenting risks, could enable greater pluralism and self-determination for other nations.

The libertarian perspective, common in publications like Reason magazine, frames American decline partly as the consequence of big-government policies at home and overreach abroad. Libertarian thinkers argue that America's military interventions, empire of bases, and participation in endless wars have drained national resources and created enemies unnecessarily. The solution, from this view, is returning to a more limited role in international affairs, focusing on trade rather than military power, and reducing government's domestic footprint to restore economic dynamism.

Some independent analysts, particularly at think tanks like the Quincy Institute for Responsible Statecraft, argue that American decline stems from the failure to adapt to a changing world. The United States, they argue, continues pursuing a post-Cold War strategy of primacy based on military dominance even as the world has moved beyond the situation that made such a strategy viable. What's needed, from this perspective, isn't more spending on military power but a fundamental reorientation toward diplomatic engagement, multilateral cooperation, and finding ways to manage great power competition without constant brinkmanship.

International media sources, particularly from the Global South, often frame American decline in terms of Western hegemony more broadly. Publications from Asia, Africa, and Latin America note that American dominance represented just one form of Western colonial and neocolonial influence, and that China's rise, along with the growth of regional powers, simply represents a shift toward a more balanced and equitable global system. While not necessarily welcoming Chinese influence, these perspectives emphasize that American exceptionalism was always more myth than reality, and that the United States' relative decline simply reflects the reassertion of other civilizations' historical roles.

The Way Forward: Adaptation or Renewal?

The question facing Americans, and the world, is whether American decline represents the end of an era or the beginning of a necessary transition. Will the United States adapt to a multipolar world, finding new ways to exercise influence through cooperative leadership rather than dominance? Or will it double down on primacy, attempting to maintain unipolarity through military strength and economic coercion even as the world evolves around it?

The path of adaptation would require acknowledging changed realities and redefining American leadership for a new era. This might mean reforming international institutions to give other nations greater voice, accepting limits on American prerogatives, and focusing on areas where American leadership provides genuine added value, innovation, cultural influence, and cooperative problem-solving on climate change, pandemics, and other global challenges. It would require repairing domestic institutions, rebuilding trust, and demonstrating that democracy can deliver better results for ordinary citizens.

The path of renewal, conversely, would assert that American strength remains unique and that decline is a choice rather than inevitability. This approach would involve massive investments in domestic priorities, infrastructure, education, research, and manufacturing, to restore economic competitiveness. It would mean recommitting to alliances while making them more reciprocal and less one-sided. It would require cultural renewal, addressing the social divisions and loss of shared purpose that undermine democracy's effectiveness. And it would mean reasserting American leadership, but perhaps in different forms: leading by example in democratic governance, economic innovation, and solving global problems.

The reality likely lies between these poles. The United States will remain a dominant power for the foreseeable future, but not the uncontested hegemon of the post-Cold War era. Its influence will need to be exercised in more cooperative, less unilateral ways. Domestic renewal will require both policy responses to concrete problems and cultural reconciliation of divisions. And the multipolar world emerging will test whether nations can cooperate on shared challenges even as they compete for advantage.

What remains certain is that the United States cannot simply return to the status quo of the 1990s. The world has changed, and America has changed with it. The decline, the real and perceived erosion of power, influence, and confidence, represents an invitation to reimagine America's role in a new century. Will that reimagination result in constructive adaptation, bitter decline, or some other future yet to be imagined? The answer depends not just on what America does, but on who Americans believe they are and what they want their country to be.
