Russia's Claims That the CIA and Mossad Orchestrated Terrorism: A Disinformation Investigation

Published: May 17, 2026 Disinformation · Intelligence · Global Security

In February 2026, a graphic circulated widely across social media platforms showing a portrait of an unnamed Russian official against the backdrop of the Russian flag, accompanied by a bold headline: "RUSSIA CLAIMS FOREIGN INTELLIGENCE CIA AND MOSSAD ARE BEHIND ALL TERRORIST ATTACKS." The image, produced by an outlet called "Daily Headlines 360," distilled into a single viral frame one of the most persistent and dangerous narratives emanating from the Kremlin's information warfare apparatus — the claim that Western intelligence agencies, rather than extremist groups or homegrown radicals, are the true architects of global terrorism. The claim is not new. It is not supported by evidence. And yet it continues to find audiences across the internet, amplified by networks linked to Russian intelligence, adopted by far-right communities, and repeated by state media in Iran and China. This investigation traces the origins, evolution, and real-world consequences of a conspiracy theory that serves as one of Moscow's most effective weapons in its ongoing information war against the West.

The Most Recent Escalation: Putin's 2026 FSB Assembly Address

The claim that Western intelligence services orchestrate terrorist attacks received its most authoritative airing in recent months on February 24, 2026, when Russian President Vladimir Putin addressed the annual assembly of Russia's Federal Security Service, the FSB, in Moscow. Standing before the leadership of Russia's primary domestic intelligence apparatus, Putin made the accusation directly. "There is no doubt that, as in recent years, Ukrainian special services and their foreign curators are behind most of them," he said, referring to the increase in crimes of a terrorist nature recorded in Russia the previous year. The phrasing was deliberate and carefully constructed. "Foreign curators" is a Kremlin euphemism for Western intelligence agencies — the CIA, Britain's MI6, and Israel's Mossad among them. Putin offered no evidence to support the claim, nor did he specify which attacks he was referencing or which foreign services he was accusing.

The address came at a moment of heightened tension. Russia had experienced a series of security incidents in early 2026, including drone strikes on infrastructure in Tuapse and elsewhere. Putin used the FSB assembly to frame these events not as the consequences of the war Russia had started in Ukraine, but as evidence of a Western conspiracy to destabilize Russia through terrorism. He called for "strengthening anti-terrorist security for energy and transport infrastructure, as well as public gathering places" and emphasized the need to protect classified military and strategic information. The subtext was clear: Russia was under attack not merely from Ukraine, but from the entire Western intelligence ecosystem, and only a vigilant security state could protect the homeland.

Two months later, on April 28, 2026, Putin returned to the theme at a meeting on election security. "The risks of terrorist threats are growing," he warned, before accusing Ukraine and its "patrons" of switching "to openly terrorist methods." Again, the "patrons" went unnamed but clearly referred to the United States and its allies. The accusation was embedded within a broader narrative: that Russia's war in Ukraine is not an act of aggression but a defensive struggle against a West that employs terrorism as a tool of statecraft. This framing serves a dual purpose — it explains away Russia's security failures by attributing them to powerful external enemies, and it justifies ever-greater internal repression as necessary counterterrorism.

The Crocus City Hall Attack and the Birth of a False Narrative

The modern iteration of Russia's "CIA and Mossad did it" narrative can be traced to a specific event: the March 22, 2024, terrorist attack on Moscow's Crocus City Hall concert venue, in which gunmen killed 143 people and set fire to the auditorium in one of the deadliest terrorist attacks on Russian soil in decades. The Islamic State Khorasan Province, known as ISIS-K or ISKP, an Afghanistan-based affiliate of the Islamic State, claimed responsibility for the attack within hours. U.S. intelligence agencies confirmed the claim. Amaq News Agency, which is affiliated with the Islamic State, published graphic video of the attack apparently filmed by one of the attackers, as reported by the Financial Times and other outlets. Eight suspects, mostly nationals of Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan, were charged with terrorism offenses.

The facts, however, were inconvenient for the Kremlin. Russian intelligence had been warned. The U.S. Embassy in Moscow had issued a public security alert on March 7, 2024, advising American citizens to avoid large gatherings over the following 48 hours due to "imminent plans by extremists to target large gatherings in Moscow." This warning was issued under the U.S. intelligence community's longstanding "Duty to Warn" directive, which requires intelligence agencies to warn both U.S. and non-U.S. persons of impending threats of intentional killing, serious bodily injury, or kidnapping. The information had been shared with Russian authorities through official channels. Putin himself had publicly dismissed the warning, calling it "provocative" and suggesting it was an attempt to intimidate Russians.

When the attack occurred anyway — when the warning Putin had dismissed proved devastatingly accurate — the Kremlin faced a profound embarrassment. The narrative pivot came swiftly. On March 25, 2024, Putin acknowledged that "radical Islamists" had carried out the attack, but insisted that Ukraine and its Western backers were linked to it. He presented no evidence. The next day, FSB Director Alexander Bortnikov went further, explicitly claiming that the United States, the United Kingdom, and Ukraine were behind the attack. He too offered no evidence. Security Council Secretary Nikolai Patrushev, one of Putin's closest allies, was asked by reporters whether Ukraine or the Islamic State was behind the attack. "Of course Ukraine," he answered, according to the state news agency RIA Novosti. Foreign Ministry spokesperson Maria Zakharova claimed the United States' initial rejection of any Ukrainian involvement was suspicious and asserted that the Islamic State itself was created by the West.

As analysts at the Institute for the Study of War noted, "Putin and other senior officials have not fully coalesced around the false narrative that Ukraine somehow conducted the March 22 attack." The oscillation between blaming Ukraine outright one day and then avoiding the issue the following day, the ISW observed, "suggests that the Kremlin has not yet established a templated line on how to discuss the attack, likely partially as a result of the shock felt by the Russian elite in its aftermath." Over time, however, the narrative hardened. By late 2024 and into 2025, the claim that Western intelligence services were behind terrorist attacks in Russia had become a standard element of Kremlin rhetoric, repeated at FSB assemblies, security council meetings, and foreign ministry briefings with increasing regularity.

The Disinformation Ecosystem: How a Lie Travels the World

The "Daily Headlines 360" graphic that recently brought the CIA-Mossad terrorism claim to renewed attention is itself a product of a much larger disinformation ecosystem. Research by multiple organizations, including the Jerusalem Post, Reuters, Bloomberg, and the European Union's disinformation monitoring bodies, has documented how Russian-linked online networks systematically create and amplify false claims about Western intelligence agencies and terrorism. The networks operate through a layered structure that ranges from official state statements — Putin's FSB address, Zakharova's briefings, Lavrov's diplomatic remarks — through state-aligned media outlets like RT, Sputnik, and RIA Novosti, down to covert influence operations that produce fabricated content designed to appear as if it originates from credible Western sources.

The most prominent of these covert networks, known by names such as Doppelganger, Matryoshka, and Storm-1516, have been extensively documented by cybersecurity researchers, intelligence agencies, and investigative journalists. Bloomberg's 2026 investigation into Storm-1516 identified more than 190 fabricated stories the group had produced since August 2023, with the network operating at twice the rate in the first quarter of 2026 compared to the same period the previous year. The group is believed to include former employees of Yevgeny Prigozhin's Internet Research Agency — the notorious "troll factory" that interfered in the 2016 U.S. presidential election — and has been linked to Russia's military intelligence agency, the GRU. Ukrainian intelligence says GRU Unit 29155, led by Oleg Kushnir, paid for the group's servers and artificial intelligence tools.

The methods these networks employ are increasingly sophisticated. Storm-1516 produces fake videos featuring forged documents and false statements from purported witnesses and victims, frequently presented in the format of a news report from a credible outlet. Audio and video are processed using artificial intelligence to create deepfakes that impersonate legitimate broadcasters. In February 2025, ahead of Germany's national election, Russian-linked networks spread faked spy agency warnings of terrorist attacks in Germany, including fake videos purporting to be from France 24 relaying warnings from French security services, and similar warnings purporting to come from the CIA and Mossad. No such warnings had been issued. The Jerusalem Post reported that cybersecurity experts working with German human rights organizations found the posts had been interacted with some 2.5 million times, with the volume tripling in the week before the election when a group named Storm 1516 joined the campaign.

During the opening days of the Iran War in late February and March 2026, the Matryoshka campaign — a related but distinct Russian disinformation operation — was documented by the security intelligence firm SecAlliance producing fake videos impersonating Le Point, Euronews, the French Directorate-General for External Security, and even the Institute for the Study of War. These fabricated videos promoted narratives undermining Western governments, damaging Ukraine's reputation, and inflaming social tensions across Europe. The pattern was consistent: high view counts, sometimes reaching a million views per post, paired with minimal organic engagement, suggesting the visibility was artificially inflated through coordinated inauthentic amplification using bot accounts.

The SVR as Propaganda Instrument

A critical element of Russia's information warfare that distinguishes it from simple propaganda is the role of the Foreign Intelligence Service, the SVR, in spreading disinformation. The Ukraine Crisis Media Center's Hybrid Warfare Analytical Group published a comprehensive analysis in February 2026 documenting how the SVR systematically participated in information operations against Ukraine and its European partners throughout 2025. The report identified five major disinformation operations in which the SVR played an active role, each following a consistent pattern.

The SVR's statements were framed as "warnings about threats," yet they contained no actual intelligence information. The agency did not name specific individuals, did not provide exact dates, and did not present any evidence. Instead, the claims were built around vague formulas such as "according to available data" or "according to available information." This framing allowed Russian intelligence-linked propagandists to create a desired information background while avoiding responsibility for the credibility of their claims. The SVR claimed Ukrainian security services were preparing terrorist attacks against Russian diplomatic missions in EU countries — no EU law enforcement agency confirmed any such preparations. The SVR claimed NATO was preparing military pressure on the separatist enclave of Transnistria — no such plans existed. The SVR claimed Ukraine was preparing a provocation at the Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant — a facility under the de facto control of Russian troops, meaning any incident would necessarily occur under Russian occupation.

The common logic across these operations, as the analytical group documented, was consistent: the SVR did not attempt to persuade audiences through facts but instead systematically constructed the image of Ukraine as a dangerous, unpredictable, and radical actor. The narrative was imposed that supporting Kyiv automatically increases risks to European security. Each message was tied to a specific political context — elections in Moldova, debates within EU countries about military assistance to Ukraine, international attention to the security of nuclear facilities, or domestic discussions in France about military deployments. By manipulating public fears, the SVR sought to undermine the willingness to support Ukraine while simultaneously legitimising Russia's own aggressive policy as a "forced response to threats."

From 4chan to the Mainstream: The Conspiracy's Journey

The claim that the CIA and Mossad control the Islamic State and orchestrate terrorism did not originate with the Kremlin. It has deeper roots in the conspiratorial underworld of the internet, particularly on the far-right message board 4chan, where it has circulated for years. Researcher Joshua Bowes of the Global Network on Extremism and Technology documented in detail how, in the immediate aftermath of the Crocus City Hall attack, 4chan's "politically incorrect" board was flooded with posts asserting that the Islamic State was a "CIA/Mossad sock puppet for terrorist attacks." One user wrote: "ISIS is just the CIA/Mossad sock puppet for terrorist attacks. I'm sure it's just a coincidence that church and mosque shooting/bombings happen all the time but if it's a synagogue it's stopped." Another asserted: "I'm inclined to believe it was ISIS, they do work for mossad/cia, that's a proven fact."

What made the Crocus City Hall aftermath distinctive was how quickly these fringe conspiracy theories migrated from 4chan to mainstream social media platforms. Bowes documented hundreds of comments on Instagram posts by major news organizations like ABC News, with comments such as "ISIS = CIA" receiving 122 likes, "So it was America?" receiving 97 likes, and "ISISrael" receiving 97 likes. A comment reading "ISIS = Israel Usa" accumulated 183 likes and 19 replies. The migration from fringe to mainstream was algorithmic as much as it was social — when conspiratorial comments receive likes, they rise to the top of comment sections, becoming more visible to all users. The algorithmic amplification of conspiracy theories creates a self-reinforcing cycle in which engagement drives visibility, and visibility drives further engagement.

The relationship between these organic conspiracy theories and the Kremlin's official narrative is symbiotic. The Kremlin does not need to invent the claim that the CIA and Mossad orchestrate terrorism — it merely needs to amplify and legitimate it. When Putin tells the FSB that "foreign curators" are behind terrorist attacks, he provides official sanction to a conspiracy theory that already circulates widely online. When Zakharova claims the Islamic State was "created by the West," she echoes and elevates the "ISIS = CIA" meme from 4chan to the level of official state discourse. The flow works in both directions: Kremlin statements provide content for social media conspiracists to amplify, while organic conspiracy theories provide the Kremlin with pre-existing narratives that resonate with audiences already inclined to believe them.

The Russian, Iranian, and Chinese Information Axis

Russia is not alone in promoting the narrative that Western intelligence agencies orchestrate terrorism. NewsGuard's analysis of the Crocus City Hall aftermath identified 150 articles from Russian, Chinese, and Iranian state media published between March 22 and March 26, 2024, that mentioned the "West" and the Moscow attack, demonstrating an apparent unified front in coverage. While blaming the West was a common thread, the disinformation narratives were tailored to specific domestic audiences. Russia blamed Ukraine; Iran pinned the attack on the United States and Israel; China hinted at U.S. responsibility without making direct accusations.

Iranian state media were particularly aggressive in promoting the CIA-Mossad terrorism narrative. The state-run Iranian Students News Agency carried a cartoon of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu carrying the Islamic State flag, stating that "Western propaganda immediately points the responsibility to ISIS" and that "obviously, the role of the Zionist regime cannot be neglected in the recent crime." Iran's flagship Islamic Republic News Agency claimed that "anti-security projects by actors such as the United States and Israel" provide the Islamic State with "the intelligence and operational support for these actors to commit bloody terrorist attacks in various countries from Iran to Russia." Iran did not provide any evidence to support these claims, nor did it acknowledge the irony that the United States had also warned Iran ahead of the January 2024 IS suicide bombings in Kerman — a warning Tehran similarly dismissed as evidence of Western complicity rather than cooperation.

China's contribution to the narrative was more subtle but no less significant. The Chinese Consul General in Osaka shared a video of a 2017 interview on Russian state outlet RT in which an American journalist and author named Paul L. Williams claimed that the Islamic State is a "CIA creation" and that "we still direct them." Williams has never claimed to be a CIA agent; his government experience is limited to his self-described role as a former consultant for the FBI advising on organized crime and terrorism. He has a documented history of making baseless claims about terrorist activity, including allegations that al-Qaeda members stole radioactive material from McMaster University in Ontario — a claim dismissed as "false" by the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission. Yet the Chinese diplomat presented Williams' assertions as authoritative, and the narrative spread rapidly across Chinese social media platforms.

The coordination among these three state media ecosystems reflects what researchers describe as a "shared narrative architecture" — not a single centrally directed campaign, but a convergence of interests that produces mutually reinforcing messaging. Russia seeks to discredit Ukraine and the West; Iran seeks to discredit Israel and the United States; China seeks to portray American power as hypocritical and destabilizing. Each state tailors the message to its own priorities, but the core claim — that Western intelligence agencies orchestrate terrorism — serves all three governments' strategic interests simultaneously.

Homegrown Terrorism: The Inconvenient Truth

The most damaging rebuttal to Russia's claims about Western-orchestrated terrorism comes not from Western officials but from within Russia itself. In June 2024, an attack on police, churches, and a synagogue in Dagestan killed 20 people and wounded 46 — the third successful terrorist attack in Russia in as many months. This time, the terrorists turned out to be relatives of major figures from the Dagestani elite, including members of Putin's own United Russia party. The domestic origin of the attackers created an unexpected fracture in the Kremlin's narrative, with several propagandists and even former officials breaking ranks to acknowledge Russia's internal problems.

Former Deputy Prime Minister Dmitriy Rogozin, now a senator for the occupied Zaporizhzhia oblast, wrote on his Telegram channel: "If we attribute every terrorist attack involving national and religious intolerance, hatred, and Russophobia to the machinations of Ukraine and NATO, then this pink fog will lead us to big problems." His post was immediately quoted by Tsargrad, a far-right television station, and the Military Review website, which is close to the Ministry of Defense. Military analyst Aleksandr Staver wrote on the site: "It is high time to recognize that terrorism has religious and national roots." Another analyst, Viktor Biryukov, warned that radical Islamism extends far beyond isolated groups in Dagestani villages, affecting nearly all spheres of society, including prisons where "supporters of radical Islam not only feel comfortable but are recruiting supporters."

Even the Nezygar Telegram channel, closely associated with the Presidential Administration, published a post by journalist Anastasia Mironova warning that "our country has nurtured an environment that ensures the full production cycle of an Islamist terrorist attack." Igor Dmitriev, a pro-Kremlin political scientist and orientalist, acknowledged that "the Special Military Operation cannot compete ideologically with Islam because Islam is a religion, and the SMO is a leadership mistake." These admissions, extraordinary by the standards of Russia's normally monolithic information space, pointed to a reality that the Kremlin's "foreign curators" narrative is designed to obscure: the roots of terrorism in Russia are substantially domestic, driven by the same factors that the Kremlin's war in Ukraine has exacerbated — the normalization of violence, the empowerment of radical groups, the marginalization of ethnic minorities, and the deliberate cultivation of Chechnya's Ramzan Kadyrov as an autonomous power center immune to federal law.

The CEPA analyst Kseniya Kirillova documented how the Kremlin's flirtations with foreign extremist groups — including Hamas and the Taliban — have further blurred the lines between counterterrorism and complicity. On May 27, 2024, the Russian Foreign Ministry and Ministry of Justice reported to Putin that the Taliban could be removed from the list of terrorist organizations. Putin reacted favorably, saying he would discuss it with his partners. The contradiction is stark: Russia officially accuses the West of orchestrating terrorism while simultaneously considering the delisting of the Taliban — which seized power in Afghanistan by force and has documented ties to extremist networks — as a legitimate negotiating partner.

The Real-World Consequences of a Lie

The claim that the CIA and Mossad orchestrate terrorism is not merely a matter of competing narratives or diplomatic posturing. It has real and measurable consequences for global security, democratic processes, and the lives of ordinary people. When Russian-linked disinformation networks spread fake CIA and Mossad terror warnings ahead of Germany's 2025 election, the goal was explicitly to "sow fear and depress voter turnout," as cybersecurity experts working with German civil society organizations documented. The messaging seemed designed to help the Russia-friendly Alternative for Germany party, as Felix Kartte, a misinformation specialist at the Mercator Institute, explained: "A scared society is much more sensitive to authoritarian narratives."

The consequences extend beyond elections. When Vice President JD Vance publicly repeated a fabricated claim originating from Storm-1516 — that Zelensky's associates had purchased two yachts worth $75 million using money stolen from Western military aid — it demonstrated how Russian disinformation can penetrate the highest levels of Western government. Former U.S. Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene also shared the story. Neither Vance nor Greene acknowledged that the claim had been fabricated by a Russian intelligence-linked network and debunked by the yacht brokers involved in the sales. The incident illustrated a fundamental vulnerability of democratic societies: when political leaders repeat foreign disinformation, they lend it a credibility that no amount of fact-checking can easily undo.

The CIA-Mossad terrorism narrative also creates a dangerous feedback loop in international relations. When Russia accuses Western intelligence agencies of orchestrating terrorism, it creates a pretext for its own escalatory actions — increased domestic repression, cyberattacks against Western infrastructure, sabotage operations in Europe, and the justification of Russia's own shadow war against the West. The CSIS analysis of Russia's "shadow war" documents how the Kremlin has employed assassination, sabotage, and cyber operations across Europe while maintaining plausible deniability. The accusation that the CIA and Mossad engage in terrorism provides rhetorical cover for Russia's own use of terrorist tactics abroad, creating a moral equivalence that benefits Moscow regardless of the facts.

Perhaps most dangerously, the narrative erodes the possibility of genuine intelligence cooperation on counterterrorism. The United States has repeatedly demonstrated its willingness to share terrorism threat intelligence even with adversaries, under the Duty to Warn directive. The U.S. warned Russia ahead of the Crocus City Hall attack. The U.S. warned Iran ahead of the Kerman bombings. In both cases, the warnings were dismissed or portrayed as evidence of complicity rather than cooperation. If Russia succeeds in convincing its population and international audiences that Western terrorism warnings are actually evidence of Western involvement, the possibility of future cooperation on counterterrorism becomes vanishingly small — and the next time a warning is issued, it may be ignored again, with potentially catastrophic consequences.

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The claim that the CIA and Mossad created, control, or direct the Islamic State has been exhaustively investigated and repeatedly debunked by credible researchers and intelligence agencies. The Islamic State's origins trace to Abu Musab al-Zarqawi, a Jordanian national who established al-Qaeda in Iraq following the 2003 U.S.-led invasion. After al-Zarqawi's death in a U.S. airstrike in 2006, the group evolved through several iterations before breaking with al-Qaeda and declaring a caliphate in 2014 under Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi. Al-Baghdadi was killed in a U.S. special operations raid in 2019. The group's affiliate, ISIS-K, emerged in Afghanistan's Khorasan Province and has carried out attacks in Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan, and Russia — targets that directly contradict the claim that the group serves Western or Israeli interests.

There is no evidence that the United States created the Islamic State or that it controls the group. The organization grew out of other groups that can be traced to a radicalized Jordanian national, according to experts at Stanford University's Center for International Security and Cooperation. The claim that the CIA armed or funded ISIS — sometimes based on U.S. programs that armed Syrian rebel groups fighting against both ISIS and the Assad regime — conflates separate programs and ignores the fundamental adversarial relationship between the United States and the Islamic State, which has been the target of a years-long U.S.-led military campaign that killed tens of thousands of its fighters and destroyed its territorial caliphate.

The claim regarding Mossad is equally unsupported by evidence. Israel has been the target of Islamic State-inspired and directed attacks, and the group has called for violence against Jewish communities worldwide. The argument that because the Islamic State has not attacked Israel directly it must be controlled by Mossad is a logical fallacy that ignores the group's strategic priorities — which have focused on establishing and defending its territorial caliphate, attacking "near enemies" in the Muslim world, and combating "far enemies" in the West and Russia — as well as the practical reality that Israel's security apparatus makes attacks within its borders exceptionally difficult to carry out.

White House National Security Council spokeswoman Adrienne Watson stated directly in response to the Crocus City Hall aftermath: "The U.S. Government shared this information with Russian authorities in accordance with its longstanding 'duty to warn' policy." Ukraine denied any involvement. British Foreign Secretary David Cameron called Russia's claims "utter nonsense." The evidence points clearly to ISIS-K as the perpetrator — a conclusion supported by U.S. intelligence, confirmed by the group's own claim of responsibility and the publication of video evidence, and consistent with the group's documented capability and history of operations.

The Daily Headlines 360 and the Social Media Amplification Problem

The "Daily Headlines 360" graphic that recently brought renewed attention to the CIA-Mossad terrorism claim exemplifies a broader challenge in the information environment. Facebook users have flagged Headlines 360 as a misinformation source, and it operates in a gray zone of social media news production — producing content that mimics the format of legitimate news while lacking the editorial standards, sourcing, or accountability of credible journalism. The outlet's graphic used visual conventions associated with credible news — a formal portrait, national flag imagery, a bold headline — to present an unsupported claim as an established fact. The social media icons in the corner of the graphic indicate that it was designed specifically for viral distribution across platforms.

This is not an isolated phenomenon. NewsGuard's analysis has documented how a Moscow-based global "news" network has infected Western artificial intelligence tools worldwide with Russian propaganda. The European Union has sanctioned multiple entities associated with Russian information manipulation, including the Foundation to Battle Injustice, which European authorities believe is linked to Storm-1516's content production. The EU has also listed two entities over information manipulation activities connected to Russian hybrid threats, as announced in April 2026. The problem is structural: social media platforms' algorithms are optimized for engagement, not accuracy, and content that provokes strong emotional reactions — outrage, fear, suspicion — receives disproportionate distribution regardless of its truthfulness.

The "Daily Headlines 360" graphic encapsulates what RAND Corporation analyst Ryan Bauer described as Russia's "firehose of falsehood" propaganda model. The model's effectiveness, Bauer wrote, stems not from the credibility of any individual claim but from the volume and variety of claims, which create an environment where "as far-fetched as it may seem to those in the West, the Kremlin's claim that the recent terrorist attack on a Moscow concert hall was orchestrated by Ukraine and Western powers may be convincing to many Russians." The same volume-based approach, amplified by social media algorithms, extends the narrative's reach far beyond Russia's borders to audiences who may encounter the claims without the context needed to evaluate them critically.

Intelligence Experts Weigh In

Current and former intelligence officials from multiple countries have expressed alarm at the spread of the CIA-Mossad terrorism narrative. The consensus among Western intelligence agencies is unequivocal: there is no evidence that the CIA or Mossad has created, controlled, or directed the Islamic State or any other major terrorist organization. The claim is regarded as a textbook example of a reflexive control operation — a concept central to Russian military doctrine in which an adversary is manipulated into making decisions that benefit the manipulator by feeding them false information that shapes their perception of reality.

The concept of reflexive control is particularly relevant because it explains why the CIA-Mossad terrorism narrative is designed not merely to deceive but to paralyze. If audiences believe that the CIA and Mossad are behind terrorist attacks, they are less likely to trust Western intelligence warnings about genuine terrorist threats, less likely to support counterterrorism cooperation with Western agencies, and more likely to view Russia's own aggressive actions as justified responses to Western provocations. The narrative doesn't need to convince everyone — it merely needs to create enough doubt and confusion to prevent the formation of a clear, fact-based consensus.

Max Hess, a fellow at the Foreign Policy Research Institute and author of "Economic War: Ukraine and the Global Conflict Between Russia and the West," told CNBC that "such conspiratorial thinking in Russia, sadly, is something that has been rather endemic to society there since the collapse of the Soviet Union, and is something which isn't going to go away and Putin will seek to feed on this." Hess added a critical observation: "I also think we should be clear that there's probably a good chance that Putin himself genuinely believes some of this paranoid thinking. His level of paranoia is something that I think hasn't been properly appreciated in the last few years. When it comes to dealing with Putin, we have to understand that, more and more, he lives in his own reality."

The danger of a leader who genuinely believes his own disinformation is that it eliminates the possibility of rational de-escalation. If Putin truly believes that the CIA and Mossad are orchestrating terrorist attacks against Russia, then no amount of diplomatic reassurance or intelligence sharing will alter his calculus. The narrative becomes self-justifying: any Western statement denying involvement is treated as further evidence of a cover-up, while any Western action — including genuinely cooperative gestures like terrorism warnings — is reinterpreted as proof of complicity. This closed-loop reasoning is the hallmark of conspiratorial thinking, and it has become embedded in the operational logic of the Russian state.

The Historical Context: A Long Pattern of Projection

Russia's accusations that Western intelligence agencies orchestrate terrorism are not merely a response to recent events but part of a long historical pattern of what analysts call "mirror imaging" or "projection" — accusing adversaries of doing what you yourself are doing or would like to do. The Soviet Union routinely accused the CIA of fomenting terrorism and insurgency, even as the KGB supported terrorist groups and insurgencies worldwide. The pattern has continued under Putin's Russia, where the government accuses the West of the very tactics it employs through its own intelligence services.

The CSIS analysis of Russia's "shadow war against the West" documents a systematic campaign of assassination, sabotage, cyberattacks, and influence operations conducted by Russian intelligence services across Europe and beyond. The Washington Post reported in May 2026 that Russia is "ramping up its attempts to kill opponents in Europe," with intelligence officials from multiple countries describing an escalation in Russian-directed violence on European soil. CNN reported that the Kremlin has tightened security around Putin himself amid assassinations and coup fears. The EU has sanctioned entities connected to the DHL parcel bomb plot, in which Russian operatives were accused of planting explosive devices in cargo shipments bound for Western targets.

The accusation that the CIA and Mossad orchestrate terrorism thus serves a dual function: it deflects attention from Russia's own use of terrorist tactics abroad, and it creates a moral equivalence that normalizes those tactics. If "everyone knows" that Western intelligence agencies blow things up and kill people, then Russia's own assassination campaigns, sabotage operations, and cyberattacks become unremarkable — just business as usual in a world where all major powers behave the same way. The narrative doesn't need to be believed in its specifics; it merely needs to erode the distinction between Russian aggression and Western defense, between the actions of democratic states governed by law and the actions of authoritarian regimes unconstrained by it.

What Can Be Done: Countering the Narrative

Countering a narrative as deeply embedded and widely amplified as the CIA-Mossad terrorism claim requires a multi-layered approach that addresses both the supply and demand sides of the disinformation ecosystem. On the supply side, continued exposure and documentation of Russian disinformation networks remains essential. The work of organizations like NewsGuard, SecAlliance, the European Digital Media Observatory, and the Hybrid Warfare Analytical Group at the Ukraine Crisis Media Center has been invaluable in identifying specific operations, attributing them to their sources, and documenting their methods and reach. Bloomberg's investigation into Storm-1516 and the EU's sanctions against associated entities represent concrete steps toward imposing costs on disinformation operations.

Social media platforms bear a significant responsibility. The persistence of accounts like "Johnny Midnight" on X — which published almost 60 fabricated stories and had more than 630,000 followers before being temporarily banned and then reinstated with no comment from the platform — illustrates the failure of content moderation systems to address state-sponsored disinformation effectively. The pattern documented by SecAlliance, in which fabricated videos receive hundreds of thousands of views but almost no organic engagement, provides a clear signature of coordinated inauthentic amplification that platforms could use to detect and remove such content proactively.

On the demand side, media literacy and critical thinking remain the most important long-term defenses. Understanding that the "Duty to Warn" policy explains why the United States alerts other countries — including adversaries — to imminent terrorist threats, and that such warnings represent cooperation rather than complicity, is essential for countering the narrative that advance knowledge of an attack proves involvement in it. Recognizing the hallmarks of disinformation — vague sourcing ("according to available data"), absence of evidence, emotional provocation, and the framing of all events as evidence of a single comprehensive conspiracy — can help audiences evaluate claims more critically.

International cooperation is also critical. The coordinated response of EU member states in sanctioning Russian disinformation entities, the sharing of intelligence about Russian influence operations among Five Eyes and European partners, and the development of common frameworks for identifying and countering disinformation represent important progress. But the speed and sophistication of Russian operations — producing fabricated content within hours of breaking events, using AI to create deepfakes, and exploiting algorithmic amplification to reach millions — means that defensive measures will always lag behind offensive capabilities unless significant investments are made in real-time detection and response.

The Bigger Picture: Why This Matters

The claim that the CIA and Mossad are behind all terrorist attacks is more than a conspiracy theory. It is a weapon in an ongoing information war, one that Russia is waging with increasing sophistication and intensity. The narrative serves multiple strategic purposes simultaneously: it explains away Russia's security failures, justifies internal repression, undermines Western credibility, erodes support for Ukraine, depresses voter turnout in democratic elections, creates pretexts for Russian escalation, and establishes a moral equivalence between democratic and authoritarian states. The fact that it is unsupported by evidence does not diminish its effectiveness; in many ways, the absence of evidence is itself a feature, because it allows believers to fill the void with their own fears and suspicions.

The "Daily Headlines 360" graphic that brought this narrative to renewed attention is a small but instructive example of how disinformation works in practice. A single image, designed for viral distribution, distills a complex geopolitical situation into a sensational claim that confirms pre-existing biases and provokes strong emotions. It requires no evidence, offers no nuance, and leaves no room for the kind of careful analysis that would reveal its fundamental dishonesty. And yet it reaches millions of people who may never encounter the careful documentation of NewsGuard, the technical analysis of SecAlliance, or the investigative reporting of Bloomberg and Meduza that debunks it.

The challenge for democratic societies is not merely to debunk individual false claims — though that remains necessary — but to build an information environment in which disinformation finds less fertile ground. This means investing in independent journalism, supporting media literacy education, holding social media platforms accountable for the amplification of state-sponsored lies, and maintaining the transparency and credibility of democratic institutions that make conspiracy theories less compelling. It means recognizing that the claim "the CIA and Mossad are behind all terrorist attacks" is not a claim about terrorism at all — it is a claim about reality, and the struggle over whose version of reality prevails is one of the defining contests of our time.

The facts, in the end, are not complicated. The Islamic State and its affiliates are real terrorist organizations with documented histories, organizational structures, and ideological motivations. They have killed Americans, Europeans, Israelis, Iranians, Afghans, and Russians alike. The CIA and Mossad have spent years fighting these groups, at great cost in lives and resources. Russia's claims to the contrary serve Russian interests, not the truth. And every time a social media user shares a "Daily Headlines 360" graphic accusing Western intelligence agencies of mass murder, they become — knowingly or not — a participant in an information war that seeks to destroy the very possibility of shared factual understanding on which democratic governance depends.
