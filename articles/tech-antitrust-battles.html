Tech Industry Antitrust Battles: The Global War Over Digital Economy Control

Major regulators challenging tech giants across multiple continents, with different countries taking radically different approaches to reigning in digital monopolies.

By Tony Wolf April 12, 2026
Analysis

The world's most powerful technology companies are under siege. Not from competitors or market forces, but from governments. Across the United States, the European Union, China, India, Brazil, and dozens of other jurisdictions, regulators are pursuing antitrust cases against Google, Apple, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft with an intensity not seen since the breakup of Standard Oil or the landmark case against Microsoft in the 1990s. But what makes this moment fundamentally different from earlier eras of antitrust enforcement is that there is no global consensus on what the problem is, let alone the solution. Each region is pursuing its own approach, shaped by its own political economy, its own relationship with these companies, and its own vision for how the digital economy should be governed.

The result is a fragmented regulatory landscape where the same company can face structural breakup demands in one jurisdiction, modest behavioral remedies in another, and explicit government partnership in a third. Where the United States is pursuing sweeping monopoly cases through its court system, the European Union has enacted an entirely new legislative framework to govern digital markets ex ante, before competitive harm occurs. Where China fines its domestic tech champions and subjects them to political rectification programs, India is building its competition enforcement capacity to extract penalties from foreign platforms. And underneath it all, the geopolitical rivalry between the United States and China has made antitrust enforcement a tool of industrial policy, with each side accusing the other of using regulation to gain advantage in the race for artificial intelligence supremacy.

The United States: Monopoly Law Meets the Courts

The United States has pursued the most aggressive antitrust enforcement against Big Tech in a generation, but its approach is defined by the slow, uncertain machinery of the federal court system. Two landmark cases against Google have produced the most significant antitrust rulings against a technology company since the Microsoft case of the late 1990s, and both reveal the enormous difficulty of applying century-old antitrust law to digital markets.

In August 2024, Judge Amit Mehta of the US District Court for the District of Columbia issued a ruling that shook the technology industry. After a lengthy trial in United States v. Google LLC, the court found that Google had illegally monopolized the general search services market and the general search text advertising market in violation of Section 2 of the Sherman Act. The evidence was damning. Google had paid billions of dollars annually to Apple, Samsung, and other device manufacturers and carriers to ensure that Google Search was the exclusive default search engine on their devices and browsers. These agreements, known as default search agreements, guaranteed that the vast majority of users would never encounter a meaningful choice about which search engine to use. The court found that these exclusive agreements were exclusionary conduct that effectively foreclosed rivals from competing on the merits.

The internal communications revealed during trial were particularly striking. Google executives had described their goal in terms that left little room for interpretation about competitive intent. In one email, a senior executive stated the goal was to "crush" the competition. In another, a Google employee compared the company's position in the advertising technology stack to "Goldman Sachs or Citibank owning the NYSE," an analogy that crystallized the conflict of interest at the heart of Google's ad tech operations. These were not the words of a company competing on the quality of its product. They were the words of a company using its market power to ensure competitors never got the chance to compete at all.

The remedies phase of the search case, however, revealed the enormous gap between finding a violation and crafting an effective remedy. The Department of Justice initially proposed structural remedies including the forced divestiture of Google's Chrome browser, which serves as a critical distribution channel for Google Search. But the final remedies order, issued in late 2025, stopped short of structural breakup. Instead, the court ordered behavioral remedies including the termination of exclusive default search agreements, requirements for user choice screens, and data sharing provisions designed to enable rivals to compete more effectively. Google would retain Chrome. The behavioral remedies, while significant, illustrated the inherent limitation of the US antitrust approach: courts are reluctant to impose structural breakup on companies whose products are deeply integrated into the daily lives of hundreds of millions of users, and behavioral remedies are notoriously difficult to monitor and enforce over time.

The second major case against Google focused on its advertising technology business, and the outcome was even more consequential. In United States v. Google LLC (Ad Tech), Judge Leonie Brinkema ruled on April 17, 2025 that Google had violated the Sherman Act by monopolizing the publisher ad server market and the ad exchange market, and by unlawfully tying its dominant publisher ad server (DoubleClick for Publishers, or DFP) to its ad exchange (AdX). The court found that Google had used its vertically integrated position across the entire advertising technology stack to disadvantage rivals and maintain its dominance, and that this conduct had "substantially harmed" publishers who sell advertising space and the competitive process overall.

The ad tech case was significant because it addressed a market that directly affects the economics of the internet. Google's advertising tools are used by millions of websites and publishers to sell advertising space and by millions of advertisers to buy that space. By controlling both the tool that publishers use to sell ads (the ad server) and the marketplace where those ads are bought and sold (the ad exchange), Google occupied a position analogous to a stock exchange that also operated as the largest buyer and seller on its own platform. The inherent conflict of interest was clear to everyone except Google, which argued that its integrated services benefited the entire ecosystem. The court disagreed, finding that Google had exploited its dual role to suppress competition and extract supra-competitive fees from both publishers and advertisers. Google announced its intention to appeal the ruling, setting the stage for years of further litigation.

At the state level, antitrust enforcement has also produced results. Texas led a coalition of states in a lawsuit against Google over its advertising technology practices, and in May 2025 the parties reached a $1.375 billion settlement. While substantial in absolute terms, this amount represented a fraction of Google's annual advertising revenue, which exceeds $200 billion. For critics, the settlement illustrated a fundamental problem with antitrust enforcement in the United States: the penalties, even when they reach into the billions, are often too small to change corporate behavior or deter future violations. When a company generates hundreds of billions in annual revenue from the very practices found to be illegal, a settlement of even a few billion dollars can be absorbed as a cost of doing business.

The European Union: From After-the-Fact Enforcement to Proactive Regulation

While the United States relies on litigation under existing antitrust law, the European Union has taken a fundamentally different approach. After years of bringing traditional competition cases against Big Tech, the EU concluded that the pace of digital market dynamics made after-the-fact enforcement inadequate. By the time a competition case reaches a conclusion, the market has often already tipped irreversibly in favor of the incumbent. The remedy arrives too late to restore competition. This recognition led to the Digital Markets Act, which came into full effect in 2024 and represents the most ambitious attempt anywhere in the world to govern digital markets through proactive regulation rather than retroactive enforcement.

The DMA designates certain large platforms as "gatekeepers" based on quantitative thresholds of revenue and user numbers. As of early 2025, the European Commission had designated six companies as gatekeepers: Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, ByteDance, Meta, and Microsoft. Being designated a gatekeeper comes with a set of obligations designed to ensure that these platforms cannot use their entrenched position to disadvantage competitors and users. These obligations include requirements to allow third-party interoperability with messaging services, to allow users to install third-party app stores and apps, to refrain from self-preferencing in ranking and display of products and services, and to provide data portability for end users and business users.

The first enforcement actions under the DMA came in April 2025, and they immediately revealed the political tensions surrounding digital regulation. The European Commission found Apple non-compliant with DMA obligations regarding its App Store and issued a fine of 500 million euros. The Commission found that Apple's so-called "steering rules" violated the DMA by preventing app developers from informing users about alternative purchasing options outside the App Store. Apple had implemented what critics called "scare screens," warning messages that appeared when users attempted to use alternative payment methods or download apps from third-party sources. These screens framed external purchases as security risks, discouraging users from exploring options that would avoid Apple's commission fees of up to 30 percent on in-app purchases.

Apple had also introduced a Core Technology Fee of 0.50 euros per app install per year for apps distributed through alternative app stores once they exceed one million annual installs. Developers argued that this fee effectively penalized success and made alternative distribution economically unviable for many apps. Furthermore, the process for setting up alternative app stores on iOS devices required 15 separate steps, a labyrinthine procedure that critics argued was designed to discourage adoption rather than facilitate genuine choice. The Commission found that these measures collectively violated the DMA's requirements for fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms for alternative app distribution.

Meta was also found in violation of the DMA, receiving a fine of 200 million euros. The Commission determined that Meta's "pay or consent" model for its social media services in the EU was non-compliant. Under this model, users were presented with a binary choice: either pay a monthly subscription fee to use Meta's services without personalized advertising, or consent to the use of their personal data for targeted advertising. The Commission found that this binary choice did not constitute meaningful consent because it failed to offer a less personalized but still free option. Users were effectively forced to either pay for access to social media platforms that had become essential tools for communication and commerce, or surrender their personal data for commercial exploitation. This, the Commission concluded, did not represent the genuine choice that the DMA was designed to protect.

The fines imposed, while significant in absolute terms, were well below the maximum penalties the DMA allows. The Act permits fines of up to 10 percent of a company's total worldwide annual turnover for non-compliance, which could amount to tens of billions of euros for the largest gatekeepers. The Commission's decision to impose fines of 500 million euros on Apple and 200 million euros on Meta, representing a tiny fraction of their annual revenue, was widely interpreted as a calibrated response influenced by political considerations. Specifically, the Trump administration had explicitly threatened tariff retaliation against countries that imposed regulations perceived as targeting American technology companies. In February 2025, President Trump signed an executive order directing US trade officials to investigate whether foreign digital regulations constituted unfair trade practices. The European Commission's relatively modest fines appeared to reflect an awareness of this geopolitical pressure, seeking to enforce the DMA while avoiding a full-scale transatlantic trade conflict.

Despite these initial enforcement actions, the DMA's implementation has not been without challenges. The European Court of Justice struck down the Commission's approach to investigating mergers below the turnover thresholds of the EU Merger Regulation using Article 22 referrals, a decision that limited the Commission's ability to scrutinize acquisitions by big tech companies that fall below traditional notification thresholds. This gap has become particularly significant in the context of artificial intelligence partnerships. Microsoft's $13 billion investment in OpenAI and Amazon's $8 billion investment in Anthropic have been structured as minority investments rather than outright acquisitions, potentially avoiding merger review in both the United States and Europe. Critics argue that these partnerships give big tech companies significant influence over AI development without triggering the regulatory scrutiny that a full acquisition would require. The Commission has opened investigations into whether Microsoft's relationship with OpenAI constitutes a de facto merger, but the ECJ ruling has complicated this analysis.

As of early 2026, over 60 antitrust cases against Big Tech companies were pending in Europe, reflecting both the scale of the competitive concerns and the institutional commitment to enforcement. The Commission has also issued new preliminary findings of non-compliance against Apple regarding its terms for alternative app distribution, suggesting that the first round of enforcement did not produce the changes the Commission expected. Meanwhile, the Commission closed its investigation into Apple's browser choice screen after Apple made changes to comply, demonstrating that the DMA can produce behavioral changes even when fines are relatively modest.

China: Regulatory Rectification and Political Control

China's approach to antitrust enforcement against its technology sector is fundamentally different from anything seen in the West, because it operates at the intersection of competition law and political control. The State Administration for Market Regulation, or SAMR, is the primary antitrust enforcement agency, but its decisions are inextricable from the Chinese Communist Party's broader objectives for managing the technology sector. What emerged in 2021 was a sweeping regulatory campaign that produced the largest antitrust fines in Chinese history and fundamentally reshaped the relationship between the state and the technology industry.

The most prominent case was the enforcement action against Alibaba. In April 2021, SAMR imposed a fine of 18.2 billion yuan, approximately $2.51 billion, on Alibaba for violating the Anti-Monopoly Law through its practice of "choose one from two" exclusive dealing. This practice required merchants on Alibaba's e-commerce platforms to sell exclusively on Alibaba and not on rival platforms like JD.com or Pinduoduo. Merchants who listed products on competing platforms faced penalties including reduced traffic, lower search rankings, and in some cases removal from Alibaba's platforms entirely. SAMR found that this practice eliminated competition and harmed both merchants and consumers by reducing choice and innovation.

The Alibaba fine was the largest antitrust penalty in Chinese history and sent a clear signal that no company, regardless of its economic importance or political connections, was beyond the reach of antitrust enforcement. But the enforcement action was also understood in the context of a broader regulatory crackdown on China's technology sector that included actions against ride-hailing company Didi Chuxing, online education companies, and gaming companies. The message was unmistakable: technology companies that had grown powerful enough to challenge state priorities would be reined in.

Alibaba subsequently entered a three-year rectification program under SAMR supervision. In August 2024, SAMR announced that Alibaba had completed the rectification program, having terminated its exclusive dealing practices and implemented compliance measures. The completion of the program was widely interpreted as a signal that the regulatory crackdown was easing and that the government was returning to a more supportive stance toward the technology sector, particularly as economic growth slowed and the trade war with the United States intensified. The technology sector was needed to drive innovation and economic growth, and the government appeared to be recalibrating its approach from restriction to regulated support.

Meituan, China's largest food delivery platform, was also fined 3.4 billion yuan, approximately $469 million, in 2021 for similar exclusive dealing practices. As part of its rectification, Meituan committed to investing 1 billion yuan in profit-sharing with merchants, a remedy that blended competition enforcement with social policy objectives in a way that has no parallel in Western antitrust practice. The Chinese approach to remedies goes beyond simply stopping anticompetitive conduct to actively restructuring commercial relationships in ways that serve broader social goals.

Since 2024, China has continued to develop its antitrust framework with new regulations that reflect the growing sophistication of its enforcement apparatus. SAMR increased merger notification thresholds, which had the effect of bringing more transactions below the reporting threshold but also signaled a calibration of enforcement resources. The agency issued Horizontal Merger Review Guidelines that brought greater clarity to how mergers would be evaluated. Most significantly, the Internet Regulation Provisions took effect, representing China's first comprehensive set of rules specifically addressing unfair competition in online markets. These provisions target practices including deceptive user interfaces, forced data collection, and algorithmic manipulation that have been difficult to address under traditional antitrust frameworks.

SAMR has also been exercising its call-in powers for mergers that fall below notification thresholds but raise competitive concerns. The agency reviewed and imposed conditions on acquisitions including the Synopsys acquisition of Ansys and the JX Nippon Mining and Metals acquisition of Tatsuta Electric Wire, demonstrating that below-threshold transactions will not escape scrutiny when they affect important markets. China also brought its first data monopoly enforcement case, fining Sumscope for monopolizing financial data, an action that signals the government's intent to apply antitrust principles to data as a competitive asset, not just traditional goods and services.

The Supreme People's Court has also weighed in, issuing a new Civil Judicial Interpretation on monopoly disputes that clarifies the standards for private antitrust litigation. In a notable case, Li v. Didi, the Supreme People's Court ruled that ride-hailing did not constitute a separate relevant market and that Didi was not dominant, a decision that highlighted the ongoing difficulty of market definition in digital markets even in a jurisdiction where the government has substantial influence over outcomes. As US-China trade tensions continue to escalate, the technology sector is expected to remain a focus of Chinese antitrust enforcement, not merely for competition reasons but as an instrument of industrial policy in a global technology competition that shows no signs of abating.

Brazil: Building a Framework from Scratch

Brazil represents a different kind of challenge in the global antitrust landscape: a large and rapidly growing digital economy where competition regulation is still being built. In September 2025, the Brazilian government announced the Digital Brazil Agenda, a comprehensive policy framework that includes the Digital Fair Competition Bill, designated as PL 4675/2025. This legislation would establish a new regulatory framework within CADE, Brazil's competition authority, specifically designed to address the competitive challenges of digital markets.

The proposed framework draws heavily on the European Union's Digital Markets Act, adapting the gatekeeper model to the Brazilian context. It would establish a designation process for "platforms of systemic relevance" based on quantitative thresholds similar to those used in the DMA. Designated platforms would face obligations including transparency requirements, interoperability mandates, and non-discrimination rules. The legislation also addresses data portability, algorithmic transparency, and restrictions on self-preferencing.

President Lula explicitly cited these regulatory measures in his address to the United Nations General Assembly, framing Brazil's digital competition agenda as part of a broader Global South effort to assert sovereignty over digital markets that have been shaped predominantly by foreign technology companies. This framing reflects a growing recognition among emerging economies that the digital economy's structure, determined largely by American and Chinese technology giants, may not serve their development interests and that proactive regulation is necessary to ensure their domestic companies and consumers are not disadvantaged.

Brazil's regulatory ambitions have not gone unnoticed by the United States. The Trump administration imposed tariffs on Brazil, citing concerns over freedom of expression, a justification that many observers interpreted as a proxy for opposition to digital regulation that could affect American technology companies. President Trump explicitly threatened economic retaliation against countries seeking regulatory alternatives to the American model of digital governance. This threat highlighted a fundamental tension in global antitrust enforcement: when regulation of digital markets becomes intertwined with trade policy, countries with less economic power may face pressure to adopt weaker regulatory frameworks than they would otherwise choose.

India: Aggressive Enforcement Against Foreign Platforms

India has emerged as one of the most aggressive antitrust enforcement jurisdictions against foreign technology platforms, using its Competition Commission to pursue cases against Meta, Apple, Amazon, and Google with increasing frequency and severity. The CCI fined Meta 25 million dollars for privacy policy violations that the commission found to be an abuse of dominant position, establishing the principle that forcing users to accept comprehensive data collection as a condition of using a service can constitute an antitrust violation, not merely a privacy concern.

Perhaps most dramatically, Apple could face fines of up to 38 billion dollars from the Indian antitrust regulator if ongoing investigations find that its App Store practices constitute an abuse of dominant position. This figure, which would represent a staggering penalty, reflects the CCI's willingness to pursue enforcement actions with financial consequences proportionate to the scale of the competitive harm. The investigation focuses on Apple's requirement that iOS app developers use its in-app payment system and pay commissions of up to 30 percent, the same practices that have drawn scrutiny from the European Commission under the DMA.

The CCI has also pursued antitrust investigations against Amazon and Flipkart, a Walmart-owned e-commerce platform that dominates the Indian market. These investigations focus on allegations that the platforms favor their own private-label products over those of independent sellers, engage in deep discounting that undercuts competitors, and enter into exclusive arrangements with smartphone manufacturers that disadvantage rival platforms. The Indian approach combines elements of both the US litigation model and the EU regulatory model, using the CCI's investigative and enforcement powers while also developing a Digital Competition Bill that would establish ex-ante regulations similar to the DMA. India's approach is notable for its willingness to impose significant penalties on foreign companies and for its framing of competition enforcement as a tool for protecting domestic economic interests in a digital economy dominated by foreign platforms.

The AI Dimension: Antitrust as a Geopolitical Weapon

The most consequential dimension of the global antitrust landscape may be the one that is least developed in existing law: the regulation of artificial intelligence. The partnerships between big tech companies and AI startups have created a new category of competitive concern that existing antitrust frameworks were not designed to address. Microsoft's $13 billion investment in OpenAI, Amazon's $8 billion investment in Anthropic, and Google's ownership of DeepMind represent concentrations of AI capability that could determine the economic and geopolitical balance of the coming decades.

The structural characteristics of AI markets make them particularly susceptible to monopolization. Training large language models requires enormous computational resources, primarily in the form of specialized chips from Nvidia, and vast quantities of data. Only the largest technology companies have the capital, infrastructure, and data access to compete at the frontier of AI development. This creates a "moat" around AI capabilities that may be even more formidable than the network effects that sustained search and social media monopolies. If a handful of companies control the foundational AI models on which the rest of the economy will depend, the competitive implications extend far beyond technology markets into virtually every sector of the global economy.

The competitive concern is not merely that big tech companies will monopolize AI development, but that they will use AI as a tool to entrench their existing monopolies. Google can integrate AI capabilities into its search engine, making it even harder for rivals to compete. Microsoft can embed AI into its Office suite and Windows operating system, leveraging its existing dominance in productivity software and desktop operating systems. Amazon can use AI to strengthen its e-commerce and cloud computing advantages. The danger is not just that AI will be monopolized, but that AI will be used to make existing monopolies impregnable.

The geopolitical dimension of AI antitrust is impossible to ignore. Both the United States and China view AI leadership as a national security imperative. American policymakers who might otherwise support aggressive antitrust enforcement against big tech companies must weigh the risk that breaking up US technology companies could hand AI leadership to China, where companies operate with explicit state support. Chinese policymakers face a mirror-image dilemma: enforcing antitrust law against domestic technology champions could weaken their global competitiveness, but failing to enforce it could allow domestic monopolies to stifle the innovation needed to compete internationally. This geopolitical framing has become a powerful argument against aggressive antitrust enforcement in the United States, and it is frequently invoked by the technology companies themselves.

The Counter-Argument: Why Some Say Antitrust Goes Too Far

The push for aggressive antitrust enforcement against big tech has not gone unchallenged. A substantial body of scholarship and advocacy argues that the current enforcement wave is misguided, that it misapplies antitrust law, and that it risks harming the very consumers and innovators it purports to protect. Understanding these counter-arguments is essential for evaluating the overall landscape.

The Cato Institute, a libertarian think tank, has been among the most vocal critics of big tech antitrust enforcement, arguing that existing competition in digital markets is robust and that the consumer welfare standard, which has guided antitrust analysis since the 1980s, does not support intervention against companies whose products are largely free to consumers. Under the consumer welfare standard, antitrust enforcement is justified only when consumers are demonstrably harmed, typically through higher prices, reduced output, or lower quality. By this metric, Google providing free search, free email, and free maps is difficult to characterize as harming consumers, even if its market share exceeds 90 percent in search.

A Stanford Law Review article titled "The Big Tech Antitrust Paradox" crystallized this tension. The paradox is that the very characteristics that make big tech companies dominant, their ability to provide free services subsidized by advertising revenue, also make it difficult to demonstrate consumer harm under traditional antitrust analysis. Consumers are not paying higher prices. In many cases, they are paying nothing at all. The harm, critics of the consumer welfare standard argue, is more diffuse: reduced innovation, less choice, the subtle manipulation of user attention and behavior, the extraction of personal data as a form of payment that users may not fully understand or consent to. But these harms are difficult to quantify and even more difficult to address through antitrust remedies.

The Chamber of Progress, a technology industry funded advocacy group, has argued that the Department of Justice's case against Google is "disconnected from economic reality," pointing to the rapid emergence of AI-powered search alternatives and the competitive pressure from Microsoft's Bing and other search engines as evidence that the market is more dynamic than the government's case suggests. The argument is that by the time antitrust remedies take effect, the market may have already transformed in ways that make the remedies irrelevant or even counterproductive. This argument has some force: the rapid rise of AI chatbots as search alternatives has introduced a new competitive dynamic that did not exist when the DOJ filed its case in 2020.

Perhaps the most politically potent counter-argument is the geopolitical one. Proponents of this view argue that breaking up American technology companies at a time of intensifying competition with China for AI leadership would be strategically reckless. The United States' competitive advantage in AI rests on the scale and resources of companies like Google, Microsoft, and Amazon. Fragmenting these companies could reduce their ability to invest in the massive computational infrastructure needed for frontier AI development, potentially ceding leadership to Chinese companies that operate with state backing and face no comparable regulatory constraints. This argument has found a receptive audience in Washington, where concerns about China's technological rise have become a unifying theme across party lines.

The technology companies themselves have also argued that their integrated ecosystems benefit consumers through seamless experiences and rapid innovation. Breaking up Google's advertising technology stack, for example, could reduce the efficiency of digital advertising and ultimately reduce the revenue that supports free online content and services. Forcing Apple to allow alternative app stores could compromise the security and privacy that iPhone users expect. These arguments are not without merit, but they also reflect the self-interest of companies that benefit enormously from the status quo.

The Fundamental Divide: What Is Competition For?

Beneath the specific cases and the competing arguments lies a fundamental philosophical divide about the purpose of antitrust law and the nature of competition in digital markets. This divide is not merely academic. It shapes every aspect of the global antitrust landscape, from which cases are brought to what remedies are imposed to how success is measured.

The American approach, rooted in the consumer welfare standard, asks whether consumers are harmed. If prices are low, quality is high, and output is increasing, the mere fact of market concentration is not in itself a basis for intervention. This approach has the virtue of analytical clarity and economic rigor, but it struggles with digital markets where the relationship between market power and consumer welfare is mediated by complex mechanisms like data extraction, attention manipulation, and the two-sided nature of platform markets. When a service is free, the price is zero, but the cost is not. The cost is paid in data, in attention, in the subtle shaping of choices and preferences. Traditional antitrust analysis is poorly equipped to account for these costs.

The European approach, embodied in the DMA, asks a different question. It asks whether the structure of digital markets, dominated by a handful of gatekeeper platforms, is inherently hostile to competition regardless of whether current consumer prices are low. This structural approach does not require proof of consumer harm in each individual case. Instead, it identifies a set of practices and structural characteristics that are presumed to be harmful and prohibits them ex ante, before competitive harm occurs. This approach is more interventionist and less economically precise, but its proponents argue that it is better suited to the dynamics of digital markets where tipping can occur rapidly and irreversibly.

China's approach adds yet another dimension: antitrust enforcement as an instrument of state control and industrial policy. The Chinese government uses antitrust law not only to promote competition but to ensure that powerful technology companies remain subordinate to state objectives, to redistribute economic benefits more broadly, and to maintain the government's ability to direct the economy toward strategic priorities. This approach produces enforcement actions that would be unthinkable in Western jurisdictions, such as requiring companies to invest in profit-sharing programs for small merchants, but it also raises concerns about the rule of law, due process, and the use of antitrust enforcement as a tool of political repression.

The emerging approaches in India and Brazil reflect the perspectives of the Global South, where the dominant concern is not merely the abuse of market power within domestic markets but the structural dependence of domestic economies on foreign technology platforms. For these countries, antitrust enforcement is partly about protecting competition and consumers but also about asserting economic sovereignty in a digital economy that was designed by and for companies based in the United States and China. The fines imposed by India's CCI and the regulatory frameworks being developed in Brazil are as much statements of independence as they are competition policy instruments.

What Happens When Jurisdictions Collide

The fragmentation of the global antitrust landscape creates practical problems that no single jurisdiction can solve. A company that is required to allow alternative app stores in the European Union may not be required to do so in the United States, creating inconsistent obligations across markets. A company fined in China for exclusive dealing practices may engage in similar conduct in other jurisdictions where enforcement is weaker. A remedy imposed in one country may be undermined by the company's ability to route around it through operations in other jurisdictions.

The most dangerous form of fragmentation is the intersection of antitrust enforcement and trade policy. When the United States threatens tariff retaliation against countries that regulate American technology companies, it transforms antitrust enforcement from a matter of domestic competition policy into a tool of economic coercion. This dynamic creates a perverse incentive structure where countries with less economic power than the United States may self-censor their regulatory ambitions to avoid trade retaliation. The result is a race to the bottom in which the most powerful country's preferences shape the global regulatory landscape, not through consensus or agreement but through economic pressure.

The Trump administration's threats against the European Union over DMA enforcement illustrate this dynamic with stark clarity. The DMA was enacted through democratic legislative processes by the elected representatives of 450 million European citizens. The fines imposed under the DMA were based on violations of law determined through administrative proceedings with due process protections. Yet the United States treated these legitimate regulatory actions as trade barriers to be countered with tariffs. If this approach becomes normalized, it would effectively give any country with sufficient economic power a veto over the domestic regulatory decisions of other countries. This is not a sustainable foundation for global governance of digital markets.

The alternative is some form of international cooperation or coordination on digital competition policy. The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development has convened discussions on competition in digital markets, and the International Competition Network provides a forum for competition authorities to share best practices. But these bodies have no enforcement power and no ability to resolve conflicts between jurisdictions. The fundamental question of whether digital markets should be governed by a single set of global rules or by a patchwork of national regulations remains unanswered, and the geopolitical tensions between the United States, China, and the rest of the world make meaningful international cooperation unlikely in the near term.

The Stakes

The global antitrust battles over tech are ultimately about something much larger than whether Google pays enough for default search placement or whether Apple charges too much for in-app purchases. They are about who controls the infrastructure of the digital economy and therefore who shapes the economic, social, and political life of the twenty-first century. The technology platforms under scrutiny are not ordinary companies. They are the gateways through which billions of people access information, communicate with each other, buy and sell goods, and participate in public life. The rules that govern these platforms will determine whether the digital economy is open and competitive or closed and monopolized.

The current moment is defined by a profound tension. On one hand, there is unprecedented global consensus that big tech companies have accumulated too much power and that existing competition frameworks are insufficient to address it. On the other hand, there is profound disagreement about what should be done, who should do it, and what the ultimate objective should be. The United States, the European Union, China, India, Brazil, and dozens of other jurisdictions are all reaching for the same problem with different tools and different goals. The result is a global regulatory experiment of enormous consequence, one whose outcome will shape the digital economy for decades to come.

What is clear is that no single jurisdiction can solve this problem alone. Digital markets are inherently global. A remedy imposed in one country can be circumvented or undermined by corporate decisions made in another. A breakup of a technology company in one jurisdiction creates complex questions about how the separated entities operate in others. And the geopolitical dimension, the fear that aggressive antitrust enforcement will cede competitive advantage to rival nations, is a powerful constraint that affects every jurisdiction's enforcement calculus. The challenge is not merely to enforce antitrust law effectively within individual jurisdictions but to develop a framework for international coordination that can address the global nature of digital market power without subordinating the regulatory sovereignty of individual nations to the interests of the most powerful countries.

The technology companies themselves have an unprecedented stake in this outcome. They have built global empires worth trillions of dollars on business models that depend on network effects, data accumulation, and platform control. Any serious attempt to address the competitive problems of digital markets will require changes to these business models that could reduce their profitability and their market power. The companies have responded with sophisticated lobbying operations, legal challenges, and public relations campaigns designed to frame antitrust enforcement as harmful to innovation and consumers. They have also structured their operations and partnerships, particularly in artificial intelligence, to avoid regulatory scrutiny while maintaining control over critical technologies.

The outcome of these battles will not be determined by any single court decision or regulatory action. It will emerge from the interaction of dozens of legal proceedings, regulatory frameworks, and political decisions across the world. It will be shaped by the ongoing tension between competition enforcement and geopolitical calculation, between domestic regulatory sovereignty and international economic pressure, between the structural power of technology companies and the democratic authority of governments. What is at stake is nothing less than the governance of the digital economy and, by extension, the governance of the economic and social life of billions of people who depend on digital platforms for their daily activities. The antitrust battles of the 2020s will determine whether the digital economy remains open to competition and innovation or becomes permanently dominated by a handful of companies that are too big to challenge and too powerful to regulate effectively.
