The AI Regulation Battle: When Human Rights Collide with Global Dominance

The EU AI Act versus America's laissez-faire approach versus China's industrial policy. Three competing visions of AI governance are reshaping the global order, with developing nations caught in the crossfire.

By Tony Wolf January 15, 2025
Investigative Report

hen Vladimir Putin declared in 2017 that "whoever becomes the leader in artificial intelligence will become the ruler of the world," he was stating what many in global capitals already believed but rarely said aloud. AI is not just another technology. It is the foundation upon which future economic, military, and cultural dominance will be built. Eight years later, that statement has become the organizing principle of a new global conflict that has almost nothing to do with traditional warfare.

The battle lines are drawn not between armies but between regulatory regimes. The European Union has enacted the most comprehensive AI governance framework in history, explicitly grounding it in human rights and democratic values. The United States has chosen a market-driven approach that prioritizes innovation over centralized regulation, relying on voluntary commitments from technology companies and sector-specific oversight. China has built a hybrid model that combines strict top-down control with strategic flexibility, regulating through selective enforcement while aggressively pursuing industrial dominance.

These three approaches reflect fundamentally different views of what AI should be and whose interests it should serve. They also represent competing visions of the future global order. The EU is betting that its human-centric approach will become the global standard, leveraging its market power through the so-called Brussels Effect. The United States is gambling that innovation-led growth will maintain its technological edge and that competitive markets will self-correct. China is calculating that state-directed development with flexible enforcement can achieve both economic dominance and social control.

What all three approaches share, however, is a fundamental blindness to the perspective of the Global South. Developing nations did not sit at the table when these frameworks were designed. They cannot realistically adopt any of them wholesale. And they face the prospect of becoming either testing grounds for unregulated AI experiments or collateral damage in the great power technology race. This report examines how we arrived at this moment, what each approach actually does, the trade-offs and contradictions within each model, and why the current trajectory risks creating a two-tier world in AI governance.

The European Approach: Rights-Based Regulation and the Brussels Effect

The EU AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024 and will be fully applicable by 2026, represents the first attempt anywhere in the world to create a comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. The European Commission describes it as a "risk-based approach" that fosters "trustworthy AI" while strengthening innovation. The reality is more complex.

The Act divides AI systems into four risk categories. Unacceptable risk AI practices are outright banned. These include social scoring systems that evaluate citizens based on social behavior or personal characteristics, manipulative AI that exploits vulnerabilities to distort behavior and cause harm, and certain forms of biometric identification and emotion recognition. High-risk AI systems in critical areas like education, employment, law enforcement, migration, and essential services face rigorous compliance obligations including risk assessment systems, high-quality data requirements, traceability, human oversight, and cybersecurity measures. Limited and minimal risk AI systems face transparency obligations or no restrictions at all.

What makes the EU approach distinctive is not just the risk classification system but the legal theory behind it. The Act treats AI as a product subject to safety regulation, then overlays fundamental rights protections. This produces a unique blend of consumer safety law and human rights doctrine. Supporters argue this is the only ethical approach to technology that can affect democratic processes, employment decisions, and fundamental freedoms. Critics warn that treating AI primarily as a threat to be contained will cement Europe's position as a laggard in the global AI race.

There is also a strategic calculation that European policymakers rarely acknowledge publicly. The AI Act is designed to create a Brussels Effect in AI governance. The term was coined by law professor Anu Bradford to describe how EU regulations become de facto global standards because multinational companies adopt them worldwide to serve the European single market. The GDPR privacy law demonstrated this effect in practice. The EU is explicitly positioning itself to become the global regulator of AI, betting that companies will accept European rules as the cost of accessing a market of 450 million wealthy consumers.

This assumption may have worked in 2016 when Europe was a comparable market share to the United States in digital services. The AI landscape has changed dramatically since then. The European Union hosts virtually none of the leading AI foundation model providers. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and the companies driving breakthrough innovation are all American. The Chinese models powering the largest population centers are Chinese. Europe is effectively trying to regulate foreign technologies with foreign companies, and the question is whether those companies will accommodate European requirements or simply decline to serve the market.

The Innovation Paradox

EU officials argue the AI Act will actually boost innovation by creating legal certainty and building public trust. The experience with GDPR, however, suggests the opposite may happen. Following GDPR's implementation, European startups raised 40 percent less venture capital than American counterparts, and the EU has not produced a single tech giant to rival American or Chinese firms. The concern is that by prioritizing rights over innovation, the EU may permanently cede technological leadership while exporting its regulatory model without building domestic capacity.

There are also implementation challenges that risk undermining the Act's effectiveness. The legislation uses intentionally vague language to maintain flexibility as technology evolves. This means much of the real regulation will happen through guidance documents, codes of practice, and enforcement decisions. The European AI Office, established in 2024 to oversee implementation, will need to interpret provisions across 27 member states with different administrative cultures and enforcement capabilities. Germany may enforce the Act rigorously while smaller countries with fewer resources struggle to build the technical expertise needed for meaningful oversight.

The Act's treatment of general-purpose AI models illustrates this challenge. Models with computational training requirements above 10 to the power of 25 floating point operations are classified as introducing systemic risk and face additional obligations. This threshold was the product of intense negotiation between those advocating strict oversight and those warning about overregulation. It reflects an attempt to find common ground, but it also means the dividing line between regulated and unregulated models is somewhat arbitrary. A slightly more powerful model trained with marginally more compute faces dramatically different requirements.

What the EU has created is a rights-based framework that represents a coherent philosophical vision. AI should serve human beings and protect democratic values. Human oversight should be embedded in systems that make consequential decisions. Transparency should prevent manipulation. These are noble aspirations, but they are also fundamentally defensive. The EU has asked what could go wrong with AI and built regulations to prevent those outcomes. What it has not wrestled with is how Europe will build the AI capabilities needed to remain economically competitive and technologically sovereign in a world where other powers are playing by different rules.

The American Approach: Market Innovation and Distributed Oversight

The United States has taken a fundamentally different path. Instead of creating a single comprehensive AI law, the Biden administration issued Executive Order 14110 in October 2023 on the "Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Development and Use of Artificial Intelligence." This order represents the most extensive AI governance initiative in American history, and its structure reveals a distinct philosophical approach.

The Executive Order does not create new regulations. Instead, it directs over 50 existing federal agencies to identify how existing laws apply to AI and to issue guidelines, standards, and best practices. The Environmental Protection Agency is directed to address AI's environmental impacts. The Department of Labor is tasked with examining workforce implications. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau must assess AI in financial services. The Department of Education is instructed to evaluate AI use in educational settings. This fragmented, distributed approach relies on domain-specific expertise rather than centralizing AI oversight in a single agency.

This reflects the American tradition of skepticism toward centralized regulatory authority. The same constitutional structure that created separation of powers and federalism produces a regulatory philosophy that spreads responsibility across many agencies and levels of government. It also reflects an assumption that innovation will emerge from competitive markets and that regulation should follow innovation rather than shaping it proactively.

There is also a recognition of political reality. Comprehensive AI legislation would require passage through a divided Congress where $46 billion in annual lobbying spending, much of it from technology companies, would focus intensely on any bill. The Executive Order allows the administration to act unilaterally while avoiding legislative gridlock. But this also means American AI governance lacks the democratic legitimacy and statutory authority of the EU approach.

The voluntary commitments framework is even more revealing. In July 2023, the White House secured pledges from leading AI companies to conduct safety testing, invest in cybersecurity, and watermark AI-generated content. These are not legally binding requirements. They are promises that companies can honor or not, change or abandon, without facing penalties. The assumption is that reputational concerns and the threat of future regulation will be sufficient to ensure compliance. Critics argue this is precisely the mistake that led to the spread of social media harms without meaningful oversight.

California's Failed Experiment

The closest America came to comprehensive AI regulation was California Senate Bill 1047, introduced in February 2024. The Safe and Secure Innovation for Frontier AI Models Act would have required developers of large-scale AI models to implement safety certification, third-party audits, and kill-switch capabilities. The bill passed the California legislature but was vetoed by Governor Gavin Newsom in September 2024. His veto message stated that the bill focused on the wrong metrics—model size and computational resources—rather than actual high-risk deployments. The debate over SB 1047 revealed deep divisions within the American technology community about how to regulate AI.

The lack of comprehensive federal legislation has pushed AI regulation to the state level. At least 40 states introduced AI-related bills in 2024, and six have passed legislation. Texas and Connecticut adopted statutes focused on preventing discrimination. This patchwork creates compliance challenges for companies operating nationally. It also means protection varies dramatically by geography, raising questions about equal protection under law.

What the American approach optimizes for is speed and innovation. By not imposing upfront regulatory burdens, it allows companies to move fast and break things. The assumption is that market competition will weed out harmful products and that sector-specific regulators can address problems as they emerge. The voluntary frameworks provide political cover without constraining development. What it does not provide is a coherent strategic vision for how America should navigate the global AI race.

There is a deeper contradiction in the American approach. While the Biden administration talks about the importance of safe and trustworthy AI, American foreign policy has simultaneously pursued aggressive technology containment strategies. Export controls on advanced semiconductor chips to China, sanctions on AI-related research institutions, and restrictions on cross-border technology transfer all position AI as a strategic asset in great power competition. This creates a split personality domestically where AI is celebrated as an innovation that benefits humanity, while internationally it is treated as a weapon to be controlled.

The most significant problem with the American approach, however, is that it simply does not work internationally. Other countries do not have America's regulatory capacity, its fragmented but highly specialized agencies, or its tradition of industry self-regulation. Without a comprehensive federal law, the United States cannot negotiate from a position of strength in international organizations. It cannot offer its approach as a model for other nations because it barely has a unified model itself. The American commitment to innovation above all else is credible, but the American claim to offer a framework for global AI governance is not.

The Chinese Approach: Developmental State with Flexible Enforcement

China's strategy for AI governance is the most sophisticated and least understood of the three major powers. Western observers often characterize Chinese regulation as purely authoritarian—command and control from a centralized government seeking surveillance dominance. This narrative misses the complexity and strategic flexibility of China's approach.

China has actually been more aggressive than the United States in enacting AI-specific regulations. A November 2021 law on data protection, a March 2022 regulation on recommendation algorithms, and January and August 2023 regulations on generative AI all came before the EU AI Act reached final form. These regulations are not comprehensive like the EU approach, but they are targeted at specific use cases as they emerge. This reflects a pragmatic, iterative regulatory philosophy that adjusts rules based on real-world experience rather than attempting to codify everything upfront.

The regulatory process itself is more consultative than many Westerners realize. Chinese AI regulations emerge from iterative processes involving mid-level bureaucrats, academics, corporations, startups, and think tanks. The central government provides overall direction aligned with Communist Party ideology, but the details emerge through stakeholder consultation. This produces regulations that reflect practical considerations about what is workable in the market, not just ideological dictates from above.

What makes the Chinese system distinctive is the gap between regulatory rules on paper and enforcement in practice. China has arguably the most onerous AI registration requirements of any country. AI providers must register models, provide detailed documentation, demonstrate compliance with content restrictions, and submit to ongoing monitoring. As of March 2024, only 546 AI models had been officially registered, and just 70 were large language models. This seems incredibly restrictive in a country with thousands of AI companies and millions of AI applications.

The reality of enforcement, however, is remarkably flexible. Large national champion companies like Baidu, Tencent, and Alibaba face full compliance requirements because they have market dominance potential and political influence. Small and medium-sized enterprises, by contrast, fly under the radar as long as they do not attract public attention. A startup operating quietly without a large public presence faces minimal regulatory scrutiny. This dual enforcement regime—strict rules on paper, flexible enforcement in practice—allows China to maintain political control while encouraging innovation from smaller players.

This flexibility is embedded in broader industrial policy. The Made in China 2025 strategy, integrated with the 13th and 14th Five-Year Plans, explicitly calls for supporting 10,000 "Little Giants"—small and medium-sized enterprises recognized as key innovation drivers. These companies receive government support and regulatory forbearance precisely because they are driving technology development. The system rewards innovation while controlling concentration of power. When companies grow too large or politically threatening, regulators step in with harsh crackdowns, as seen with Alibaba and Ant Group in 2020-2022. This creates a predictable pattern: innovate freely, grow to scale, then face tighter restrictions.

The Chinese approach also reflects a fundamentally different conception of the human-AI relationship. The EU's human-centric framework puts human rights at the center. China's socialist values framework puts social harmony and collective well-being at the center. This means AI regulations explicitly address concerns about algorithmic discrimination, workers' rights, and content that could cause social unrest. But it also means concepts like freedom of speech and individual autonomy are subordinate to collective stability. The Chinese system assumes AI will be integrated into social management and that governance is about ensuring that integration serves collectivized goals rather than individual liberation.

What China has built is a hybrid model that combines the EU's centralized rulemaking with American-style innovation incentives, but with its own ideological foundation. It is more than simply authoritarian control. It is a sophisticated mechanism for directing technological development toward state-defined priorities while maintaining flexibility to adapt as technology evolves. The question is whether this model can sustain innovation once it catches up to the frontier, or whether the fundamental tension between control and creativity will eventually become overwhelming.

The Missing Perspective: Global South Exclusion

The most significant flaw in all three major approaches to AI regulation is that none was designed with input from the Global South. Developing nations in Africa, Latin America, South Asia, and Southeast Asia are being asked to choose from regulatory frameworks created without their participation, none of which fit their realities, all while the technology accelerates through their societies.

The EU approach is simply unaffordable for most developing countries. Implementing the AI Act requires sophisticated regulatory agencies, technical expertise, enforcement capacity, and a civil society robust enough to monitor compliance. The cost of building these capabilities—training bureaucrats, establishing testing laboratories, creating legal frameworks—is far beyond what most developing nations can justify when they struggle to provide basic healthcare, education, and infrastructure. The EU is effectively saying here is a model you should adopt, but here is no funding or technical assistance to help you adopt it.

The American approach offers no coherent framework to adopt at all. The distributed regulatory system, the reliance on voluntary commitments, the state-level patchwork—this is a product of American constitutional structure and political institutions that simply do not exist in most other countries. A developing country cannot recreate the Environmental Protection Agency, the Securities and Exchange Commission, the Federal Trade Commission, and a dozen other specialized agencies just to regulate AI. The American approach assumes institutional capacity that took more than a century to build.

The Chinese approach is appealing to some developing countries because it offers a clear model of state-directed development with technology transfer built-in. Chinese AI companies are actively expanding into markets in Southeast Asia, Africa, and Latin America as part of broader infrastructure and technology initiatives. This raises its own concerns about dependence, surveillance export, and colonialism by other means. But at least China offers a path that includes technology capacity building, not just regulation without development.

The result is that developing countries face a series of bad choices. They can adopt the EU model, drain limited resources attempting compliance requirements, and still fail to build domestic AI capacity. They can try to replicate the American approach but lack the institutional foundation to make it work. They can embrace the Chinese model but risk becoming dependent on Chinese technology and norms that they had no hand in shaping. Or they can try to create their own regulatory approaches but face pressure from all three powers to conform to their standards.

The Global South is caught in an impossible position: regulate now using frameworks built for wealthy countries, or wait and become testing grounds for uncontrolled AI development.

Even worse, they face the prospect of becoming dumping grounds for AI technologies that fail to meet regulatory standards elsewhere. If the EU bans certain forms of social scoring or manipulative AI, companies might still deploy those technologies in countries with weaker enforcement. If the United States fails to regulate privacy-invasive surveillance, American technology companies might export those capabilities to autocratic regimes seeking control. The global architecture of AI governance currently has no mechanism to prevent regulatory arbitrage that shifts harmful AI applications toward vulnerable populations.

The exclusion of the Global South from AI governance discussions is not just unfair. It is strategically short-sighted. Most of the world's population lives in developing countries. Most future AI users will be in the Global South. Their experiences will shape how AI evolves, for better or worse. Designing governance systems without their participation ensures those systems will fail at scale when they encounter the complexities and constraints of low-resource environments, diverse cultural contexts, and different normative priorities.

The Weaponization Dimension

Beyond the regulatory frameworks for civilian AI applications, all three powers are actively developing AI for military purposes. This dimension adds another layer of complexity and danger to the global AI landscape.

The United States remains the leader in military AI development, with decades of investment in autonomous systems, predictive analytics, and command-and-control technologies. The Department of Defense's AI Strategy published throughout the 2020s emphasizes the importance of AI for maintaining military superiority. Ethical guidelines around responsible AI use in warfare have been developed, but they are voluntary and subordinate to operational requirements.

China has accelerated military AI development as part of its broader military modernization program. The integration of AI into surveillance, command systems, naval operations, and missile guidance systems is a stated priority. The opacity of Chinese military development makes it difficult for external observers to assess capabilities, but there is broad consensus among security analysts that China is rapidly closing the gap with the United States.

The EU's approach to military AI is constrained by its member states' varying defense commitments and historical sensitivities. While some European countries like France and Britain have significant military AI programs, the EU as an institution focuses primarily on human rights dimensions. This creates a security dilemma where EU member states must balance their commitments to human rights-based AI governance with the practical demands of national defense and alliance commitments like NATO.

The Killer Robots Debate

In December 2024, the United Nations General Assembly voted to begin formal discussions on a treaty regulating lethal autonomous weapons systems. The vote was 161 states in favor, with notable abstentions. Human Rights Watch and other organizations have called for a preemptive ban on fully autonomous weapons that can select and engage targets without human intervention. The United States and China have both expressed skepticism about such a treaty, arguing that existing international humanitarian law is sufficient. This disagreement reflects a fundamental split about whether military AI requires new governance frameworks or can be managed through existing rules.

The weaponization of AI creates regulatory contradictions that civilian frameworks cannot address. How can the EU prohibit manipulative AI in commercial applications while European militaries develop AI-enabled psychological operations? How can the United States champion safe and trustworthy AI abroad while conducting AI-enabled drone strikes in violation of international law? How can China promote socialist values in domestic AI while exporting surveillance technologies to authoritarian regimes?

These contradictions are not merely hypocrisy. They reflect the reality that AI governance cannot be separated from geopolitics. The same technology that regulates credit applications powers missile guidance systems. The same models that generate chatbot conversations enable disinformation campaigns. The same capabilities that improve healthcare efficiency also enable predictive policing. Attempting to create governance systems for "good AI" fails to acknowledge that the technology itself has no moral center—it amplifies whatever priorities its deployers choose.

The Strategic Consequences

The collision of these three regulatory approaches is producing strategic consequences that will shape the coming decades. The most immediate is the acceleration of technological fragmentation. The United States has imposed export controls on advanced semiconductor technology to China, restricting access to the chips needed to train cutting-edge AI models. China has responded with its own export controls on critical minerals and has accelerated domestic chip manufacturing. The EU has pursued digital sovereignty initiatives to reduce dependence on American and Chinese technology platforms.

This fragmentation is not slowing down AI development. It is creating parallel technological ecosystems that may eventually become incompatible. If American, European, and Chinese AI models develop according to different regulatory frameworks, cultural values, and technical standards, the result could be a digital Berlin Wall where technologies cannot cross borders and knowledge cannot flow. This makes it harder to achieve global cooperation on shared challenges like climate change, pandemics, and nuclear proliferation because the underlying technological infrastructure will not support it.

There is also a growing risk of regulatory races to the bottom. If one jurisdiction imposes lighter regulatory burdens to attract AI companies and achieve economic growth, others may feel pressured to match that approach to remain competitive. Conversely, if one jurisdiction imposes strict regulations that actually work without killing innovation, others may feel pressure to harmonize. The danger is that political considerations rather than evidence about what actually works will drive regulatory decisions.

Most troubling is the way these regulatory battles are embedding inequalities into the very structure of the digital future. The companies and countries that lead AI development will reap disproportionate economic returns and shape cultural and political norms. Those that fail to develop domestic capacity or appropriate regulations will become dependent on foreign technologies and subject to foreign values. The colonial dynamics of physical resource extraction are finding new forms in the digital realm, with AI serving as the new front.

What This Means

The current state of AI governance is untenably fragmented, but breaking down existing frameworks to build something more inclusive and effective seems politically impossible. Each of the three major powers has invested too much in its approach to reverse course now. The EU cannot abandon its human-rights foundation without triggering a legitimacy crisis. The United States cannot successfully impose centralized regulation without facing constitutional challenges and industry resistance. China cannot abandon state control without threatening regime stability.

What might work is not a single global framework but a recognition of plural legitimacy. Different regions and countries will legitimately choose different approaches to AI governance based on their values, priorities, and capacities. A human-rights-based model will make sense in Europe. A market-driven model may continue in America. A developmental state model will persist in China. Developing countries will create approaches that reflect their own constraints and needs.

The challenge is ensuring these different approaches can coexist without causing technological fragmentation that harms everyone and without protecting human rights only for the wealthy. This requires new international forums where diverse perspectives can be respected rather than dominated by the traditional powers. It requires technical standards that enable interoperability across political systems. It requires recognition that some regulatory issues from algorithmic discrimination to autonomous weapons require global minimum standards even if approaches vary regionally.

The window for getting this right is closing quickly. AI models are already being deployed at massive scale, embedding assumptions and priorities into global systems. Regulatory frameworks that emerge now will shape the trajectory for decades. The question is whether AI becomes a technology that serves all of humanity or a tool of control in the hands of the few, whether it reinforces existing hierarchies or helps build more just and equitable societies.

This Report's Findings

The EU AI Act, American voluntary commitments, and China's hybrid enforcement approach represent three incompatible visions for AI governance. The European model prioritizes human rights but risks ceding technological leadership. The American model prioritizes innovation but lacks strategic coherence. The Chinese model balances control with development but excludes democratic participation. All three systematically exclude the Global South from governance design. The current trajectory points toward technological fragmentation and digital colonialism. A viable path forward requires acknowledging plural legitimacy and building interoperability frameworks that enable coexistence without isolation.

We are witnessing the birth of a new digital order shaped not by neutral technology but by deliberate political choices about who gets to make those choices. The question is whether we build an order that serves everyone or one that serves the powerful. The direction we take will determine whether artificial intelligence becomes a tool for human flourishing or an engine of inequality. There is still time to choose the former, but the clock is ticking.
