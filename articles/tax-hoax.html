Claim One: Case No. 24-1791, The Ruling That Does Not Exist

The post builds its entire architecture on a specific case number: 24-1791. The implication is that this is a real, searchable Supreme Court docket number that the reader simply cannot access because it has been sealed. Let us examine that claim with precision.

The Claim
"Case number 24-1791. Filed under seal. Decided 7-2. No dissent published. No media briefing. No public docket entry."
The Reality
Case number 24-1791 does not exist as a Supreme Court case. A search of the Supreme Court's public docket, PACER (the federal court electronic records system), SCOTUSblog, and Oyez, which maintain comprehensive records of all Supreme Court filings including sealed matters, returns nothing resembling this case. The number format "24-XXXX" designates cases filed in the October 2024 term. The 2024-2025 Supreme Court term is fully documented by multiple independent tracking organizations. No income tax abolishment case appears in any of them. The only case found bearing the designation "24-1791" in federal records is a Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals opinion, an entirely different court, in an unrelated matter.

It is worth dwelling on the "filed under seal" claim, because this is a piece of legal-sounding language that does real work in the post. Sealed federal cases do exist. The government uses them in national security contexts, grand jury proceedings, and certain witness protection matters. But a Supreme Court ruling, which by its nature establishes binding legal precedent for the entire country, cannot be "filed under seal" and kept from the public indefinitely. The Supreme Court does not issue secret constitutional rulings. There is no mechanism in federal law, court rules, or American legal history for the nation's highest court to quietly abolish a constitutional amendment without any public record. The suggestion is not merely false; it is constitutionally illiterate.

When the Supreme Court has dealt with genuinely sensitive or unexpected decisions, from Bush v. Gore in 2000 to the Dobbs opinion overturning Roe v. Wade in 2022. They have been published through the Court's official opinion system, reported by the legal press, and entered into the public docket within hours of issuance. The Dobbs draft opinion was leaked months before its release and triggered a massive investigation. The idea that a ruling abolishing the entire federal income tax, affecting every American and roughly $4.7 trillion in annual government revenue, could be secretly issued and suppressed is not a conspiracy theory. It is a fantasy that misunderstands, at a fundamental level, how American constitutional law works.

Claim Two: The PACER Leak, the Clerk, the Nine Minutes, the Blockchain

This section of the post is its most cinematically constructed element. A young clerk, three months on the job, accidentally uploads the secret ruling. It sits on PACER for nine minutes. Fourteen thousand people download it. The clerk is placed on leave. The file is scrubbed. But the blockchain preserves it.

The Claim
"A clerk, 26 years old, 3 months on the job, accidentally uploaded the ruling to the public PACER system for 9 minutes before it was pulled. 14,000 downloads before the file vanished."
The Reality
There is no record of this incident anywhere. Not in news coverage of the Supreme Court, not in legal press accounts of PACER activity, not in any communications from the Supreme Court public information office, not in coverage by SCOTUSblog, Law360, Bloomberg Law, or any of the dozens of outlets that cover federal court operations closely. A genuine nine-minute PACER upload of a major Supreme Court ruling, with 14,000 downloads, would have generated immediate, massive news coverage. Fourteen thousand downloads in nine minutes represents roughly 26 downloads per second sustained for the full nine-minute window. That is not a quiet leak. That is a viral event that would have been reported within hours by every legal journalist in the country. No such reporting exists.

The "blockchain does not forget" line is a particularly cynical flourish. It is designed to preempt the obvious objection, where is the document? -- by invoking a technological concept that sounds authoritative to people unfamiliar with it. In practice, blockchain-stored documents are publicly accessible by design. If 14,000 people downloaded a Supreme Court ruling and someone stored it on a blockchain, it would be trivially easy to find and share. No such document has been produced anywhere, by anyone, on any platform. The absence is not because the document is hidden. It is because the document does not exist.

There is also the internal math to consider. The post claims the file was available for nine minutes and generated 14,000 downloads. This requires believing that either an automated download bot hit the file roughly 26 times per second for nine consecutive minutes, or that 14,000 individual humans somehow discovered, navigated to, and downloaded an obscure federal court filing in the time it takes to watch a YouTube video. Neither scenario is plausible. The numbers are not estimates or approximations. They are invented to sound precise.

Verdict

No PACER upload occurred. No clerk was placed on leave. No document was scrubbed from servers. No blockchain record exists. The entire narrative was invented. There is zero corroborating evidence from any news outlet, court observer, or legal tracking service.

Claim Three: The 16th Amendment Was Never Ratified

This is where the post makes its most legally detailed claim, and it is here that the real origins of the hoax become visible. The argument that the 16th Amendment was improperly ratified is not new. It is not even recent. It is a recycled piece of pseudolegal mythology with a specific, well-documented history, and a body count measured in prison sentences.

The Claim
"The core finding: the 16th Amendment... was never legally ratified. 4 of the 36 states that allegedly ratified the amendment submitted documents with material alterations to the text. Different punctuation that changed legal meaning."
The Reality
This argument originates from a 1985 book, "The Law That Never Was," by William J. Benson and Martin "Red" Beckman. Benson claimed that variations in wording, punctuation, and capitalization across state ratification documents voided the amendment. Courts at every level, from federal district courts to the Supreme Court itself, have uniformly, repeatedly, and emphatically rejected this argument as legally frivolous. Benson himself was convicted of federal tax evasion, sentenced to four years in federal prison, and in 2007 had a federal court rule that his "Reliance Defense Package",which he sold to tax protesters, constituted fraud. His petition to the Supreme Court was denied in November 2009. The argument the viral post recycles has been dead law for 40 years.

The legal history here is extensive and unambiguous. Courts have not merely disagreed with the non-ratification argument. They have sanctioned attorneys who advanced it, calling it "patently frivolous." The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, ruling in Benson's own criminal appeal, stated directly that "we have repeatedly rejected the claim that the Sixteenth Amendment was improperly ratified" and that "Secretary Knox's decision is now beyond review." The United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois ruled in 2007 that Benson's materials constituted a "fraud perpetrated by Benson" causing "needless confusion and a waste of the customers' and the IRS' time and resources."

The Supreme Court itself, through its 1916 ruling in Brushaber v. Union Pacific Railroad, affirmed the constitutional validity of the income tax under the 16th Amendment. That precedent has stood for 110 years. Every court that has subsequently been asked to revisit the ratification question has declined to do so and imposed sanctions on those who raised it.

What the viral post has done is take this thoroughly discredited, 40-year-old argument and repackage it as the finding of a secret 2024 Supreme Court ruling. It is, in effect, a laundering operation, taking intellectual fraud and giving it the fictitious imprimatur of the nation's highest court.

Claim Four: $4.7 Trillion Was Used to Suppress Cancer Cures

Having established its fictional legal premise, the post pivots to a second, equally fabricated claim: that the federal income tax has functioned not as government revenue but as a mechanism to suppress medical cures. The post names Royal Rife and his "frequency machine" as the paradigmatic example of a cure that was "destroyed" by tax-funded institutional suppression.

The Claim
"Royal Rife's frequency machine, destroyed by the AMA in 1939. Funded by your tax dollars. The NIH spent $41 billion last year. Zero went to frequency healing."
The Reality
Royal Raymond Rife (1888-1971) was an American inventor who developed a high-powered microscope and claimed in the 1930s that specific radio frequencies could kill cancer cells and bacteria. His claims were investigated at the time and were not reproducible by independent researchers. No peer-reviewed study has ever validated the claim that Rife's device cures cancer. PolitiFact, Cancer Research UK, the American Cancer Society, and WebMD have all reviewed the evidence: there is none. The AMA did not "destroy" the Rife machine. The device's claims simply did not hold up under scientific scrutiny. Rife machines are still sold today. They are not suppressed technology, and the FDA classifies them as unproven medical devices. The claim that "zero" NIH funding goes to frequency-based or bioelectric research is also false: NIH has funded bioelectromagnetics research for decades through multiple institutes.

The Royal Rife mythology is one of the oldest and most durable fixtures in alternative medicine conspiracy culture. It has been circulating in various forms since the 1980s, when author Barry Lynes published "The Cancer Cure That Worked," a book making claims about Rife that researchers have since documented as historically inaccurate in numerous respects. The Rife machine narrative persists not because the evidence supports it, but because it is emotionally powerful: it offers the possibility that someone you loved who died of cancer could have been saved, that there was a cure, and that it was taken from you. This is an extraordinarily effective emotional hook, and it has been used to sell unproven devices, dangerous treatment regimens, and false hope to desperate people for four decades.

The post's broader claim, that $4.7 trillion in annual federal revenue has been systematically directed toward suppressing medical cures rather than toward government operations, does not survive contact with basic budget arithmetic. The federal government's $4.7 trillion in annual spending (a figure that is roughly accurate for recent fiscal years) goes to Social Security ($1.4 trillion), Medicare and Medicaid ($1.5 trillion), national defense ($800 billion), interest on the national debt ($600 billion), and a range of other programs including actual medical research. The NIH budget, at roughly $47 billion annually, funds tens of thousands of legitimate scientific studies. The claim that this entire apparatus exists to bury cures is not an alternative theory of government finance. It is a statement that requires ignoring everything publicly documented about how federal agencies operate.

Claim Five: The 83-Day Countdown and the Transition Plan

Perhaps the most psychologically manipulative element of the post is its countdown: "83 days remain." This number is presented as a specific timeline derived from the fictional ruling's "pages 71 through 89," which supposedly outline a transition plan giving the executive branch 180 days to prepare. The clock, the post claims, started 97 days ago.

The Claim
"The court gave the executive branch 180 days to prepare. That clock started 97 days ago. 83 days remain. When the seal lifts, the IRS dissolves."
The Reality
There is no countdown. There is no transition plan. There is no 180-day window. There is no sealed ruling from which a clock could have started. The numbers were chosen because a specific countdown creates urgency and gives readers a concrete future date to anticipate, a technique borrowed directly from doomsday and "prosperity gospel" content, both of which have long used artificial timelines to sustain engagement. When 83 days pass without an IRS dissolution, the post's authors will simply not acknowledge it. The claim will be updated, revised, or replaced with a new version. This is how these narratives survive falsification.

The countdown mechanism is worth examining in some detail because it reveals the post's primary purpose. Genuine breaking legal news does not come with a countdown to when it will affect your paycheck. Real court rulings take effect immediately and are reported immediately. The countdown serves a different function: it keeps the reader emotionally engaged, gives them a date to share with others, and creates a built-in excuse for why the promised transformation has not yet occurred. "The seal hasn't lifted yet." "They're still preparing." "The 83 days aren't up." By the time the countdown expires, most readers will have forgotten the specific claim, and the post's authors will have moved on to the next fabrication.

The Real History of the 16th Amendment

Because the post relies on distorting actual history. It is worth setting out what that history actually shows, including its genuinely complicated parts, which the post's authors have apparently not read or do not care about.

The 16th Amendment was proposed by Congress in 1909 and ratified in 1913. It reads in full: "The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States, and without regard to any census or enumeration." The amendment was necessary because the Supreme Court had struck down a federal income tax law in 1895 (Pollock v. Farmers' Loan and Trust Co.), ruling that income taxes constituted a "direct tax" that had to be apportioned by state population under Article I of the Constitution. The 16th Amendment removed that restriction.

When William Benson traveled to state archives in 1984 and found variations in wording and punctuation across different states' ratification documents. He was not discovering evidence of fraud. He was discovering what historians and archivists already knew: that in the early twentieth century, state legislatures often paraphrased federal text in their own resolutions rather than transcribing it verbatim, that clerical errors were common in handwritten documents, and that none of this affected the legal validity of the ratification under the established rules governing the process. Secretary of State Philander Knox reviewed these variations at the time of ratification, found them immaterial, and proclaimed the amendment ratified by 38 states, two more than the 36 required. As of 1992, a congressional analysis concluded that 42 states had ratified the amendment.

Courts examining Benson's research have not simply dismissed it on procedural grounds. They have examined the underlying documents and concluded that the variations Benson identified do not constitute legally material alterations. The Seventh Circuit found that "Benson did not discover anything." The claim that punctuation differences "changed legal meaning" to such a degree that the entire amendment is void is not a legal argument that has ever persuaded any court, at any level, in any jurisdiction.

There is a legitimate, ongoing debate about federal tax policy, about rates, brackets, deductions, corporate taxation, wealth taxes, the regressive nature of certain levies, and whether the tax code serves democratic or plutocratic interests. These are serious questions with serious advocates on multiple sides. None of them have anything to do with the claim that the amendment itself was never ratified. That claim is not a contribution to tax policy debate. It is a distraction from it, one that has historically served the interests of those who want to prevent productive discussion of actual reform.

What the Supreme Court Actually Ruled on Taxes Recently

While the post's fictional Case No. 24-1791 does not exist, the Supreme Court has issued real, significant tax rulings in recent years. Understanding them helps illustrate just how far the viral post departs from reality.

The most consequential recent Supreme Court tax case was Moore v. United States, decided in June 2024. The Moores, an American couple, challenged a provision of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act that taxed their share of undistributed earnings from an Indian company they had invested in. They argued this constituted an unconstitutional tax on unrealized income. The Supreme Court rejected their challenge 7-2, ruling that Congress had the authority to tax the income under the 16th Amendment. The ruling was significant precisely because it upheld, rather than curtailed, Congress's taxing power. Justice Kavanaugh wrote for the majority. The two dissenters were Justices Thomas and Gorsuch, and their dissent was published, as all Supreme Court dissents are, in the public record.

The Moore case attracted substantial attention from legal scholars and tax policy experts because a ruling in the Moores' favor could have threatened the constitutional basis for a wide range of taxes, including potentially the taxation of retirement accounts and partnership income. The Court's decision to rule narrowly in favor of the government's taxing power was thus a significant affirmation of existing tax law, the precise opposite of what the viral post claims.

There is no secret parallel proceeding. There is no sealed companion case. There is no 94-page ruling sitting on a blockchain waiting for its seal to lift. The actual history of recent Supreme Court tax jurisprudence moves in exactly the opposite direction from what the post describes.

Why People Share This: The Psychology of Viral Disinformation

It would be easy, and wrong, to dismiss everyone who shares content like this as simply credulous. The post is designed by people who understand human psychology reasonably well, and it exploits real grievances and real experiences.

The federal income tax is genuinely unpopular. People genuinely feel that the tax code is unfair, that wealthy individuals and corporations exploit loopholes unavailable to ordinary wage earners, that the government spends money on things they oppose, and that the system benefits those who already have power. These feelings are not paranoid. They are, in many respects, accurate. The U.S. tax code is extraordinarily complex, often regressive in its practical application, and has been shaped over decades by the lobbying of interests that do not represent ordinary wage earners. There are serious academics, economists, and policy advocates on both the left and right who have documented these failures in detail.

The viral post feeds on that legitimate anger and redirects it toward a fantasy. Instead of systemic reform, which is difficult, slow, and requires political organization, it offers the promise of a secret ruling that will fix everything in 83 days without anyone having to do anything. This is not incidentally appealing. It is engineered to be appealing. And it is more likely to be shared by people who have genuinely been failed by the system than by people who have not, because the emotional need it fills is larger.

The medical suppression narrative performs the same function for a different grievance. People who have lost family members to cancer or other serious illnesses, and who feel, often correctly, that healthcare in America prioritizes profit over outcomes, are primed to believe that cures exist but have been buried. The pain behind that belief is real even when the specific claim is false. And the post weaponizes that pain to make the tax claim more emotionally resonant: it's not just money they took from you. It's your grandmother. It's your father. It's every person you loved who died of something that could have been treated if the system had been different.

This is not new. It is a very old technique applied to new distribution infrastructure.

How to Verify Claims Like This Yourself

The post was designed to be difficult to verify for people who are not familiar with federal court systems. The "sealed ruling" framing is specifically intended to make the absence of evidence feel like evidence of suppression rather than evidence of fabrication. Understanding how to check these claims takes only a few minutes and requires no legal expertise.

The Supreme Court's public docket is available at supremecourt.gov. All cases, including those with certain sealed elements, appear in the public docket with at minimum a case number and parties' names. A search for case 24-1791 returns nothing. PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) at pacer.gov provides electronic access to all federal court records, including the Supreme Court. A free account allows public docket searches. SCOTUSblog at scotusblog.com maintains independent, comprehensive coverage of every Supreme Court case from filing through decision, including cases that involve sealed or sensitive material. It does not list anything resembling this case. Oyez at oyez.org provides similar comprehensive records with full audio of oral arguments.

For the medical claims: the NIH's Research Portfolio Online Reporting Tools (RePORTER) at reporter.nih.gov allows public searching of all NIH-funded research grants by topic, institution, and year. A search for bioelectromagnetics, bioelectric medicine, or frequency-based therapies returns hundreds of funded studies. The claim that "zero" NIH funding has gone to these areas is verifiably false in approximately thirty seconds. For the Rife machine specifically: PolitiFact, Snopes, Cancer Research UK, and the FDA have all published detailed analyses. None of them find evidence that the device works or that its suppression is the product of a coordinated institutional conspiracy rather than the ordinary process of science failing to validate an unproven claim.

The Supreme Court did not abolish income tax. Your April 15th deadline still applies.
